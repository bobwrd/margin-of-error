## Economics

### China locks in $17 billion in U.S. farm purchases
**What happened:** Reuters and AP reported that China agreed to buy at least $17 billion of U.S. agricultural goods each year through 2028, including beef and poultry access. That eases some pressure on U.S. farmers after the trade fight, but the value depends on whether the commitments hold.
**Analysis:**
1. **Shock type & market:** Positive trade-policy shock in the bilateral farm goods market. It is macro as well as sectoral because it changes export revenue, import prices, and policy expectations.
2. **Model:** Basic open-economy trade model with tariffs and non-tariff barriers.
3. **Static equilibrium:** Lower trade barriers raise demand for U.S. farm exports and lift producer prices. Chinese buyers and food processors face lower input costs if access is real and not just a headline number.
4. **Dynamic path:** If the commitment is enforced, firms will sign longer contracts and invest more in shipping, storage, and processing. If it is not enforced, risk premia stay in the price and trade volumes stay choppy.
5. **Distribution & incidence:** U.S. farmers gain first. Chinese consumers and feed users gain from cheaper supplies. Domestic Chinese producers that compete with imports lose, and short-run demand is inelastic enough that prices move more than volumes.
6. **Frictions & market failures:** The main frictions are policy uncertainty and weak commitment. Non-tariff barriers and shifting enforcement make it hard for firms to plan.
7. **Macro-financial / external transmission:** Higher farm exports support U.S. rural incomes and trim one source of bilateral tension. The external balance effect is modest but real.
**Welfare & policy:** Welfare rises if the purchase commitments are credible. A written schedule with clear enforcement would help, but it also limits room for either side to use trade as leverage.

### Thailand’s Q1 GDP beat forecasts
**What happened:** Reuters said Thailand’s economy grew 2.8% year on year in the first quarter, above the 2.2% consensus forecast. The main fact is the surprise itself: output held up better than markets expected.
**Analysis:**
1. **Shock type & market:** Positive macro output surprise. It is a broad demand and activity shock, not a narrow sector story.
2. **Model:** AS–AD. The clean reading is that aggregate demand or net external demand came in stronger than expected, or that supply held up better than feared.
3. **Static equilibrium:** Real output is higher than the forecast baseline, which improves tax receipts and corporate earnings relative to expectations. Slack is smaller than markets priced in.
4. **Dynamic path:** If the strength is coming from a one-off factor, it fades next quarter. If it reflects steadier tourism, exports, or credit conditions, it lasts and keeps policy easier for less time.
5. **Distribution & incidence:** Exporters, tourism-linked firms, and workers in those sectors gain first. Borrowers also gain if stronger growth reduces recession risk. Firms that depend on imported inputs face more pressure if demand firms up and the currency reacts.
6. **Frictions & market failures:** Forecast misses matter because Thailand, like many open economies, is exposed to external demand and commodity prices. Weak visibility on sector-level drivers makes it hard to know how much of the surprise will stick.
7. **Macro-financial / external transmission:** Better growth supports the current account and local asset sentiment. It can also delay rate cuts if the central bank thinks the economy no longer needs as much help.
**Welfare & policy:** Higher growth is welfare-improving if it comes from real demand rather than unsustainable credit. The policy trade-off is between supporting activity and not overheating the parts of the economy already near capacity.

## Finance

### Bond markets sell off as inflation fears rise
**What happened:** Reuters reported a global bond rout as oil prices climbed and inflation fears rose. Markets were also pricing a higher chance of Fed tightening by year-end, with similar pressure on European rates.
**Analysis:**
1. **Shock type & market:** Inflationary macro shock hitting sovereign bond markets and duration assets. It is persistent if energy prices stay elevated.
2. **Model:** IS–LM plus bond pricing through expectations of future short rates.
3. **Static equilibrium:** Higher expected inflation and tighter policy lift yields and cut bond prices. Long-duration assets take the first hit.
4. **Dynamic path:** If oil stays high, yields can keep rising until growth slows enough to offset the inflation impulse. If oil falls back, the term premium can compress fast and part of the selloff reverses.
5. **Distribution & incidence:** Long-bond holders, pensions, and insurers lose on mark-to-market value. Banks may gain from a steeper curve in the near term. Emerging-market borrowers face a higher dollar funding bill.
6. **Frictions & market failures:** Bond pricing is moving on a shock that may or may not last, so the market can overshoot. Liability-matching rules can force asset sales into a falling market.
7. **Macro-financial / external transmission:** Higher U.S. yields lift the dollar and tighten global dollar liquidity. That can push capital out of riskier markets and raise refinancing stress outside the U.S.
**Welfare & policy:** Welfare falls because borrowing costs rise and financial wealth is hit. Central banks can reduce some of the uncertainty through clearer reaction functions, but they cannot remove the oil shock itself.

### HSBC opens a $4 billion facility for Chinese clean-tech exporters
**What happened:** Reuters said HSBC launched a $4 billion credit facility to support Chinese firms in clean power, data centers, electric vehicles, and AI. It is a cross-border finance story with a clear sector tilt.
**Analysis:**
1. **Shock type & market:** Credit supply expansion in trade and project finance. It is mostly micro at first, but it can matter macro later if it changes external financing flows.
2. **Model:** Basic open-economy banking model with credit intermediation.
3. **Static equilibrium:** The loan line lowers funding frictions for eligible firms and can reduce borrowing spreads. That makes export and investment projects easier to finance.
4. **Dynamic path:** If the facility is used well, it can pull forward investment and export growth in transition tech. If China growth slows or policy shifts, drawdowns can stall and the line stays partly unused.
5. **Distribution & incidence:** Large Chinese firms with bankable projects gain the most. HSBC gains fee income and client ties. Smaller firms and weaker borrowers gain less because credit still flows to the best collateral and balance sheets.
6. **Frictions & market failures:** The main issue is information asymmetry about project quality and policy risk. Cross-border lending also carries regulatory and sanction risk.
7. **Macro-financial / external transmission:** The facility can support yuan-linked investment and trade settlement. It also ties HSBC more closely to China’s external sector, so any geopolitical shift can feed back into bank risk.
**Welfare & policy:** Welfare is higher if the credit goes to productive projects, not weak balance sheets rolled over for optics. Clear underwriting and ring-fenced structures would reduce contagion risk, but they also reduce flexibility.

## Law (Economics-Relevant)

### Indian court orders Apple to cooperate in antitrust case
**What happened:** Reuters reported that an Indian court told Apple to fully cooperate in an antitrust case tied to the iPhone apps market. Apple had wanted the matter paused while it challenged the penalty law.
**Analysis:**
1. **Shock type & market:** Regulatory and antitrust shock in the app distribution market. It is persistent because the case can change compliance costs for a large platform.
2. **Model:** Standard IO with platform pricing and market power.
3. **Static equilibrium:** Apple faces higher investigation and possible penalty exposure. That raises expected compliance costs and may change how it prices access to the app ecosystem.
4. **Dynamic path:** If the case keeps moving, Apple and similar platforms will spend more on legal defense, data requests, and process design. A harder enforcement stance could also push some pricing power back toward developers and consumers.
5. **Distribution & incidence:** Consumers and app developers may gain if platform behavior becomes less restrictive. Apple and investors face higher legal risk. Smaller rivals may gain if rules reduce gatekeeper power.
6. **Frictions & market failures:** The case is really about information asymmetry and platform control. Regulators need financial data to size penalties, while the firm wants to limit disclosure and preserve bargaining power.
7. **Macro-financial / external transmission:** The direct macro effect is small, but the ruling matters for digital competition and investment in India’s app economy. It can also shape how other regulators treat big platforms.
**Welfare & policy:** Welfare rises if the market is more open and penalties are tied to real harm. The trade-off is that heavier disclosure and enforcement raise compliance costs and can chill platform investment.

### Supreme Court keeps mifepristone access in place for now
**What happened:** Reuters reported that the Supreme Court stayed a Fifth Circuit ruling that would have restricted mifepristone by telehealth and mail while the case continues. The immediate effect is to preserve the current distribution channel.
**Analysis:**
1. **Shock type & market:** Regulatory shock in the pharmaceutical distribution market. It is persistent until the underlying case is resolved.
2. **Model:** Standard IO with regulated pharmaceutical access.
3. **Static equilibrium:** The stay keeps current access in place and avoids an abrupt supply break. Patients and pharmacies keep using the same delivery channels.
4. **Dynamic path:** If the stay remains or the lower-court ruling is reversed, current prescribing and mail-order systems keep working. If not, the market shifts toward in-person access, state-by-state variation, and higher search costs.
5. **Distribution & incidence:** Patients seeking medication abortion gain from the stay. Pharmacies and telehealth providers keep business continuity. States that wanted tighter limits lose immediate leverage.
6. **Frictions & market failures:** The legal patchwork creates uneven access and raises transaction costs for providers and patients. The market has to absorb shifting federal and state rules at the same time.
7. **Macro-financial / external transmission:** The direct macro effect is limited, but health-system billing and pharmacy logistics are affected. A hard restriction would raise compliance costs and could reduce telehealth volume.
**Welfare & policy:** Welfare is higher when access is stable and the rules are clear. The trade-off is that federal clarity can collide with state-level policy preferences.
