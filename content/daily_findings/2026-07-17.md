# Daily Intelligence — 2026-07-17

Date: 2026-07-17

The week’s clearest economic signal is that access is being rationed at several different gates. Central banks are watching energy and AI infrastructure costs while households face tighter credit, governments are adding court capacity because delay has a price, and platform deals are concentrating control over everyday services. The common question is who can absorb a shock when the system becomes more expensive or harder to replace.

## 1. Macro finance: inflation risk is shifting from demand to scarce inputs

Federal Reserve Vice Chair Philip Jefferson said the Fed may need to raise rates if inflation does not improve soon, although he currently sees holding short-term borrowing costs steady as sufficient. His remarks sit between two forces: the labor market remains relatively stable, while tariffs, energy prices, and the AI build-out are feeding costs into the economy. That makes the policy problem less simple than a demand slowdown. Higher rates can reduce spending, but they do not create more electricity, chips, shipping capacity, or oil.

Markets are already pricing this uncertainty. Asian shares weakened on Friday and Japan’s Nikkei fell 2.8% as oil moved toward a weekly gain amid renewed Gulf hostilities. South Korea also announced a temporary halt on new exchange-traded funds tied to certain large technology firms and raised minimum deposits for retail investors in those products. The measure is a reminder that market access is not neutral when speculative demand becomes concentrated. A higher deposit requirement protects some households from rapid losses, but it also favours investors with more liquid savings.

The distributional effect matters. A large technology firm can spread higher energy or hardware costs across customers and investors. A household cannot spread a higher transport bill or food price across a portfolio. Monetary policy therefore works through unequal balance sheets. The same rate increase that cools an asset boom can make it harder for a low-income borrower to refinance, especially when formal credit is already being withdrawn.

## 2. Development, justice, and access: credit and courts both punish delay

China’s rising consumer defaults show why account ownership is a weak measure of financial inclusion. Reuters described a 27-year-old telecoms maintenance worker in Jiangsu facing about 140,000 yuan, or $20,685, of debt across credit cards, online borrowing, and a car loan after a pay cut and the removal of a fuel allowance. China Merchants Bank reported a personal-loan non-performing-loan ratio of 1.14% in the first quarter, up 0.13 percentage point from a year earlier.

The mechanism is ordinary but severe. Several small loans can smooth a household’s spending while income is stable; after a pay cut, each repayment becomes a fixed claim on a smaller budget. Digital lenders can make borrowing quick without making the total debt burden easy to see. The result is a credit trap that reduces future consumption and leaves the borrower with less room to handle illness, unemployment, or family obligations. The better test of inclusion is whether a person can obtain emergency money without turning one shock into a chain of defaults.

Court capacity creates a similar problem through time rather than interest. The UK Ministry of Justice said it had hired more than 2,300 new magistrates since April 2025 to reduce the court backlog and delays for victims. More decision-makers may increase throughput, but staffing alone does not resolve unequal access. A person with money can pay for advice, prepare documents, and wait through adjournments. Someone facing eviction, family separation, or a benefits dispute may lose housing or income before the case is heard.

That is why California’s proposal to allow trained nonlawyers to provide limited legal assistance under the supervision of legal-aid organisations deserves attention. It treats legal judgment as a scarce service and tests whether some routine work can be delegated safely. The safeguards matter: defined services, supervision, complaint procedures, and escalation to a lawyer. Cheap document production is not the same as legal help. The economic gain comes when a person can identify the right claim, meet the deadline, and avoid an expensive procedural mistake.

## 3. Everyday markets: platform consolidation changes the price users see

Uber launched a $14.8 billion offer for Delivery Hero, a deal that would create the largest food-delivery firm outside China. Delivery Hero also agreed to sell operations in 14 markets to SSW Partners for about €1.4 billion. The immediate business case is familiar: larger networks can spread logistics and technology costs across more orders. The risk is that restaurants, couriers, and customers become more dependent on one route to the market.

Food delivery is a two-sided market. Consumers want many restaurants and short delivery times; restaurants want access to consumers; couriers need enough orders to make the work pay. A large platform can improve matching, but it can also raise the cost of leaving. Restaurants may need the platform for demand, while customers may not follow a restaurant to a smaller service. Once a platform controls discovery, payment, delivery data, and advertising space, the headline commission is only part of the economic relationship.

The same issue appears in mobile software. The UK competition regulator has proposed allowing developers to direct users to payment options outside Apple and Google app stores. The aim is to reduce fees and give developers another route to collect payment. But a lower payment fee will have limited effect if the platform still controls search ranking, default placement, security approval, and access to users. Competition improves when the rival route is easy to find and use, not merely legal on paper.

Consumers also face a comparison problem. A delivery app may show a low menu price, then add service, delivery, small-order, and subscription-related charges at checkout. A ride-hailing app can change the fare according to demand and available drivers. These prices may reflect real costs, but users often make decisions before they can see the full total. Limited attention becomes an economic input that platforms can sell or exploit. The practical remedy is simple: show the complete price early, explain the reason for material changes, and make switching possible without losing access to the underlying service.

## 4. Tech, AI, and the new political economy: access to compute is becoming state policy

Chinese President Xi Jinping used the World Artificial Intelligence Conference in Shanghai to call for a people-centred approach to AI development. The speech came as Huawei displayed a large domestic AI-computing cluster, reinforcing Beijing’s effort to build an alternative to U.S.-controlled technology. This is more than a dispute over software models. Chips, data centres, electricity, cloud access, and industrial equipment determine who can build systems and who must rent them.

When compute is scarce, the first gains go to infrastructure owners and firms with access to large pools of capital. Smaller companies may receive better tools, but they may also face higher prices, vendor dependence, and less control over the data produced by their own customers. Public agencies face a similar choice: buy access from a dominant supplier or fund their own capacity. The economic question is whether AI becomes a broadly available input or a toll road with a few owners.

The labor effects depend on how firms redesign tasks. AI can help an experienced worker complete routine analysis faster, yet the same system can turn performance into a stream of metrics used for ranking and dismissal. A recent lawsuit by former Meta employees alleges that AI-related measures were used in layoffs in a way that disadvantaged workers with disabilities or medical leave; Meta denies the claims and says people made the decisions. The dispute shows why output per worker is an incomplete measure. A firm may record higher output while shifting error risk, monitoring pressure, and job insecurity onto employees.

Governments are responding by building administrative capacity. Australia plans to establish an Office of AI inside the Department of the Prime Minister and Cabinet to coordinate policy across ministries. China is presenting AI access as part of its international economic role. The United States is examining AI’s effects on jobs, productivity, inflation, and economic statistics. Coordination can reduce conflicting rules, but it does not answer who can challenge an automated decision or share in the gain from higher output. Those rights need to be practical at the point where a worker, borrower, tenant, or consumer is affected.

Across this week’s development, everyday-market, and technology stories, control is collecting at the points where people cannot easily switch. Credit apps can bridge a shortfall and then multiply the cost of a pay cut. Courts can recognise a right while delay makes enforcement unaffordable. Delivery and app-store platforms can reduce transaction costs while charging for access to users. AI infrastructure can raise output while concentrating ownership of the inputs and shifting adjustment costs onto workers. The useful measure is therefore bargaining power under stress: who can wait, compare, appeal, or move when the system changes its price or its rules.
