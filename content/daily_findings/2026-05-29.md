# Daily Intelligence — 2026-05-29

The week's cleanest signal is that software is moving decisions earlier in the chain while the costs of bad outcomes stay with the least protected people. Courts, delivery apps, brokerage platforms, and law firms all look more automated on the front end. The hard question is who pays when the queue, the fee, or the model goes wrong.[^1][^2]

## 1) Access to justice is getting thinner at the same time demand is rising

San Francisco's main immigration court has shut down after judges were fired, retired, or resigned, and roughly 117,000 cases were moved to Concord. AP reported that the shift has made cases slower and travel harder for clients who already face long odds and little slack. That is access to justice as an economics problem: when the courthouse is farther away and the backlog is larger, the effective price of a hearing goes up even if the filing fee does not.[^1]

The same pressure shows up in the flood of AI-generated pro se filings. Bloomberg Law reported that non-prisoner pro se civil cases rose from 11% of all civil cases five years ago to 16.8% today, with AI helping people file who could not afford counsel. That widens entry, but it also pushes work onto judges, clerks, and opposing counsel who must sort noisy filings from usable ones. Cheap drafting is not the same as legal capacity, and in a crowded system it can raise friction for everyone else.[^2]

## 2) Financial inclusion is still being defined by fees and timing

Consumer Reports told the FTC that food and grocery delivery apps often hide fees in ways that make the final bill hard to see, and its prior Chicago study found four delivery apps charging 48% to 52% on top of the base cost of food and taxes. The group's comment also pushed the FTC to require all-in pricing, clearer fee labels, and an end to subscription traps and personalized pricing based on user data. That is the right frame. The issue is not whether a customer can open an app; it is whether the app turns a simple purchase into a stack of charges that only appears at checkout.[^3]

Inflation makes that stack heavier. Reuters reported that U.S. PCE inflation in April rose at the fastest pace in three years, with core PCE up 3.3% year over year. Reuters also reported that U.S. banks posted a 3.6% profit increase in the first quarter to $80.5 billion as deposits grew again and banks set aside slightly more against losses. Put together, the picture is uneven: households are paying more for basic goods, while the banking system is still healthy enough to earn through the cycle. That gap matters because a small timing miss in cash flow hurts a household much more than it hurts a large lender.[^4][^5]

## 3) Platform power keeps turning convenience into control

Uber's takeover offer for Delivery Hero is a straightforward deal story and a platform story at the same time. Reuters said Delivery Hero confirmed an indicative offer valuing it at €33 a share, and that its stock hit an 18-month high on the news. The economics are familiar: when delivery markets consolidate, the winners are usually the firms that can own more of the network and take a cut at more points in the chain. The consumer sees one screen; the platform sees a toll system.[^6]

Robinhood's new move takes that logic one step further by handing customers AI agents that can trade stocks and make credit card purchases on their behalf. Reuters noted that only 21% of leaders in a Deloitte survey said their organizations had a mature governance model for agentic AI. That means execution is getting faster than oversight. Once software is allowed to act, the main question becomes who can reverse the action, explain it, and bear the loss when it goes wrong.[^7]

## 4) Law firms and regulators are fighting over the stack beneath the interface

Kirkland & Ellis is planning to spend $500 million over three to four years on its own AI platform, starting with $100 million in 2026, based on input from 250 lawyers and more than 180 technologists. That is not a novelty bet. It is a move to control the workflow, the knowledge base, and the margin inside one of the most competitive parts of the legal market. If the platform works, the firm keeps more of the surplus inside the firm instead of renting it from outside vendors.[^8]

Regulators are moving in the same direction, but more slowly. Consumer Reports is pressing the FTC to ban personalized pricing based on personal data and to force clearer fee disclosure in delivery services. That kind of rule does not solve everything, but it does make the hidden layer harder to use against consumers. The pattern across law and retail is the same: when one side owns the data, the queue, and the fee schedule, it can shape what looks like choice from the outside.[^3][^8]

The week adds up to one simple pattern. More people can file, buy, trade, or delegate through software, but the hardest power still sits behind the interface: in the backlog, the pricing engine, the data stack, and the model owner. That is where the next fights are going to happen.[^1][^2][^3][^6][^7][^8]
