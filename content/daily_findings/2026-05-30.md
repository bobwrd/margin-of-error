# Daily Intelligence — 2026-05-30

The sharpest signal this week is that software is moving into the middle of decisions faster than the rules around it. Courts are getting more self-represented filings, banks are leaning harder on models and central-bank pressure, and platforms keep turning convenience into tolls. The issue is not whether more people can get in. It is who carries the loss when the queue, the fee, or the model goes wrong.

## 1) Access to justice is getting cheaper to start and harder to finish

Bloomberg Law reported that AI-generated pro se filings are flooding court dockets, with non-prisoner pro se civil cases rising from 11% of all civil cases five years ago to 16.8% today.[^1] That is a real access gain. Someone who could not afford counsel can now file. But filing is not the same as moving a case forward. When the volume of weak or overlong material rises, judges, clerks, and the other side absorb more sorting work. The system gets more open at the front door and more clogged in the middle.

Florida’s Supreme Court is pushing in the same direction, but from the control side. Bloomberg Law reported that it adopted a statewide policy warning lawyers and pro se litigants that filings signed by them carry a certification that cited authorities exist and are correctly cited, with sanctions possible for misuse of AI.[^2] That tells you where the pressure is. Courts are not treating AI as a novelty anymore. They are treating it as a source of bad process that has to be contained. The economics are plain: if a machine can draft at near-zero marginal cost, the bottleneck shifts to review, verification, and sanctions.

## 2) Financial inclusion still means little if credit is thin or badly priced

Reuters reported that China’s central bank told banks to boost May lending because credit weakness persists, after lending unexpectedly fell in April and household and corporate loan demand stayed soft.[^3] That is what a weak transmission channel looks like. The state can lean on banks, but if households are squeezed by higher energy costs and a bad property market, the demand side does not cooperate. A loan book can be expanded on paper while the real economy still feels tight. Inclusion, in that setting, is less about opening accounts and more about whether people can borrow without stepping into a trap.

The same problem shows up in development finance. At the Africa-France summit, Reuters reported that African leaders pushed for a rethink in how risk is priced, while analysts said Emmanuel Macron’s first-loss guarantee idea fell short because debt relief and payment pauses were still missing.[^4] That is the right lens for access. A country or small business does not just need a formal offer of finance. It needs terms that reflect its actual cash flow, not a spread that assumes away the downside. Otherwise the system keeps calling the same thing inclusion while pushing the cost into the future.

## 3) Everyday pricing is still about who controls the toll booth

The App Store fight is still a clean example. Reuters reported that the U.S. Supreme Court refused to pause an order holding Apple in contempt in the Epic case, which is tied to App Store fees and the court-ordered changes Apple has fought over.[^5] The point is not just the 30% commission history. It is that the platform controls the route into the market, the payment rail, and the rules of entry. The user sees an app. The business sees a gate.

That same logic is all over delivery and ride-hailing. Reuters reported that France’s top administrative court rejected Amazon’s challenge to minimum delivery fees on books, and said the rule is part of a long fight over digital commerce and access to local booksellers.[^6] Reuters also reported that Indonesia will cut the maximum commission ride-hailing firms can take from drivers to 8%.[^7] These are not the same policy, but they respond to the same setup: platforms can hide margin inside convenience until regulators force the split open. One market does it through delivery fees on books. Another does it through driver commissions. The pattern is the same. The screen is simple; the economics are not.

## 4) AI is moving from chat to delegated authority

Robinhood’s latest move is a good sign of where consumer finance is heading. Reuters reported that it will let customers deploy AI agents to trade stocks and make credit card purchases on their behalf, while a Deloitte survey found only 21% of leaders believed their organizations had a mature governance model for agentic AI.[^8] That is not a safe place to be. Once software can act, the main questions become: who can reverse it, who can explain it, and who eats the loss if it is wrong? The product is no longer just advice. It is delegated action.

The same shift is happening inside finance and law. Reuters reported that OpenAI gave some Japanese financial institutions access to its latest model to help prevent cyberattacks, which puts frontier AI closer to core banking security.[^9] Bloomberg Law also reported that Kirkland & Ellis plans to spend $500 million over three to four years on its own AI platform, with input from 250 lawyers and more than 180 technologists.[^10] That is a useful split to watch. In finance, models are being plugged into defense and operations. In law, firms are trying to own the stack itself. Both moves point to the same labor shift: fewer pure drafting tasks, more review, exception handling, and control over the workflow.

The pattern across the week is stable. More people can file, borrow, buy, or delegate through software, but the hard power still sits behind the interface: in the backlog, the fee schedule, the risk model, and the audit trail. That is where the next fights are going to be.
