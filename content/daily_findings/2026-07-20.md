# Daily Intelligence — 2026-07-20

Date: 2026-07-20

The week’s most revealing economic story is the collision between AI investment and household capacity. Central banks are now asking whether data-centre spending will lift productivity or keep prices high, while consumer-credit defaults in China show how quickly weak income can turn “access” to finance into a debt problem. Across courts, platforms, and financial systems, control still belongs to the party that can set the terms, delay a decision, or make switching expensive.

## 1. Development, justice, and access: exclusion can be built into ordinary administration

U.S. bank regulators warned lenders to be cautious about extending credit to undocumented workers, citing the possibility that income may be less reliable or that borrowers could be deported. The guidance is nonbinding, but its practical effect may be larger than its legal form suggests. Banks often respond to supervisory signals by tightening internal risk rules, and an applicant may never learn whether a rejection came from a formal policy, a compliance model, or a staff member trying to avoid uncertainty. A person can therefore hold an account and still be excluded from useful credit, overdraft protection, or a basic record of reliable repayment.

The same access problem appears in the courts. In Thailand, three former crew members of a ship hit in the Strait of Hormuz filed a labour-rights case against the operator, alleging unfair dismissal. The case shows how low-income workers often reach legal protection only after a cross-border event has already destroyed income and bargaining power. Travel, documentation, translation, and the cost of staying unemployed while a case proceeds all affect whether a right can be exercised. Formal jurisdiction is only the first input; affordable representation and timely procedure determine whether the right has economic value.

A separate U.S. example points to the public side of the same constraint. Supreme Court justices told Congress that threats against judges had risen sharply, with the court police force expecting a 38% increase this year after a 25% rise last year. Security spending is necessary, but it also shows how public institutions can redirect scarce resources from ordinary service capacity toward protection. For people with routine civil disputes, the relevant measure is not whether a court exists. It is whether the system can hear a case, provide understandable assistance, and produce a decision before the claimant runs out of money.

## 2. Finance and financial inclusion: more credit can mean less room to absorb a shock

China’s consumer-credit problem is a direct warning against treating approval rates as a measure of financial welfare. Reuters reported that a 27-year-old telecoms maintenance worker in Jiangsu faced about 140,000 yuan, or $20,685, of debt across credit cards, online borrowing, and a car loan after a pay cut and the removal of a fuel allowance. China Merchants Bank’s personal-loan non-performing-loan ratio reached 1.14% in the first quarter, up 0.13 percentage point from a year earlier. The important fact is not that one borrower made several choices. It is that separate lenders can each see a manageable account while the household experiences one combined liability.

Digital lenders make this aggregation problem faster. A borrower can move from one app to another before any lender has a complete view of income volatility, other balances, or informal obligations to family and lending circles. A sound inclusion test should therefore ask whether the borrower can compare the total cost, pause repayment after a documented income shock, and obtain a clear record of all active obligations. Without those safeguards, a system may expand the number of people reached by finance while shrinking their ability to handle illness, job loss, or a sudden rise in food and energy costs.

At the macro level, the European Commission is preparing measures to make cross-border bank mergers easier and reduce political interference in approvals. The stated aim is to help European banks compete with larger U.S. rivals. Scale can reduce duplicated systems and make it easier to provide services across borders, but it can also leave customers dealing with fewer institutions and more concentrated decision-making. The public test should be whether larger banks improve access and pricing for households and small firms, not whether the sector produces larger balance sheets.

## 3. Everyday microeconomics and platform power: lower fees do not remove dependence

Uber’s proposed $14.8 billion takeover of Delivery Hero would create the largest food-delivery company outside China. The deal could lower logistics costs by combining driver networks, restaurant data, and demand across markets. It could also increase the platform’s control over restaurant visibility, advertising, customer contact, and the terms offered to couriers. A restaurant may appear to have a choice between platforms, yet still depend on the one that controls the largest pool of local demand. That is a switching-cost problem, not simply a question of the commission printed on an invoice.

The legal response to Big Tech is moving from fines toward rules about how platforms must behave. The EU’s General Court rejected Apple’s challenge to its designation as a gatekeeper under the Digital Markets Act, which can expose firms to fines of up to 10% of global annual turnover. An earlier EU court decision also confirmed a €4.34 billion Google Android penalty. These cases matter because a fine imposed years after the conduct may not change a developer’s daily options. Interoperability, alternative payments, data portability, and limits on self-preferencing can matter more than the headline penalty if they make switching credible.

The UK Competition and Markets Authority has proposed allowing developers to direct users to payment options outside Apple and Google’s app stores. That would attack one part of the platform’s pricing power by reducing the ability to collect a fee on every transaction. But the outcome will depend on implementation. If a platform can bury an external-payment link, threaten ranking, or make refunds and support harder outside its own system, the formal right may have little effect. In two-sided markets, visibility and trust are part of the price.

## 4. Tech, AI, and the new political economy: infrastructure costs can spread before gains do

AI is becoming an inflation issue as well as a productivity story. At the ECB’s Sintra meeting, central bankers discussed its effects on labour markets, bank lending, financial stability, security, and electricity demand. Recent reporting also put global data-centre investment for AI at more than $700 billion in 2026, with some economists expecting memory-chip costs to rise as much as 400% from 2024 levels by year-end. The distribution is uneven: chip and infrastructure firms may capture the first gains, while households face higher prices for devices and power before wages respond.

That pressure complicates monetary policy. Federal Reserve officials are weighing whether strong demand for AI infrastructure will keep upward pressure on technology products and electricity, even as they try to protect employment. Fed Vice Chair Philip Jefferson said rates might need to rise if inflation does not ease soon. This creates a familiar distributional problem: a central bank acts on an aggregate price index, while the burden is concentrated among households that spend a larger share of income on transport, utilities, and basic goods.

The labour question is about bargaining power, not only job counts. More than 200 researchers and economists, including 15 Nobel laureates and researchers from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google, called for institutions to address the economic effects of AI and the risk of large-scale displacement. A firm can raise output per worker by removing tasks, cutting staff, or intensifying work. That is a productivity gain in the accounting sense, but it does not show whether workers receive higher pay, better schedules, or access to the training needed for the next set of tasks. Indonesia’s draft plan to use AI in its $15 billion free-meals programme makes the same point from a public-service angle: tools that predict demand or detect irregularities can improve delivery, but only if affected people can challenge errors and public agencies can explain decisions.

Across the week, the pattern was consistent. Financial access without protection can turn into stacked debt; a legal right without time, representation, or public capacity can remain unusable; a cheaper platform transaction can coexist with dependence on one intermediary; and AI investment can raise infrastructure prices before its gains reach workers. The practical question for the next week is who can compare terms, survive a delay, appeal a decision, export data, or switch providers. Those are the conditions that turn nominal access into economic power.
