# Daily Intelligence — 2026-07-22

Date: 2026-07-22

The immediate macro story is a supply shock with a distributional edge: oil prices are rising as the conflict around the Strait of Hormuz threatens energy and shipping flows, even while Asian equities rebound on demand for AI-linked chips. The same week also produced a $1.5 billion copyright settlement over AI training, new EU rules aimed at weakening Google’s control over Android and search data, and an ILO estimate that almost 80 million ASEAN workers may be exposed to generative AI. Across these stories, access depends less on whether a service exists than on who controls the terms, data, infrastructure, and ability to challenge a decision.

## 1. Development, justice, and access: legal rights are expensive when procedure is slow or opaque

The economic cost of legal access is visible in the continuing fight over litigation costs. In Australia, investor Nicholas Bolton lost an appeal in the Supreme Court of New South Wales over $1.4 million in legal costs in his dispute with Keybridge Capital. The case is not a simple measure of who was right on the underlying dispute. It shows how quickly a civil claim can become a balance-sheet contest: a party with enough resources can keep funding counsel, evidence, and appeals, while another may have to settle or stop long before the merits are fully tested.

The same problem appears in consumer credit, where the legal remedy often arrives after the financial harm. Buy-now-pay-later products are designed to make borrowing feel like a checkout choice rather than a loan. The CFPB’s earlier review found that more than 13% of surveyed BNPL transactions involved a return or dispute, with $1.8 billion disputed or returned in 2021. The current U.S. retreat from a federal BNPL framework has left states to fill the gap, with Illinois reported as the second state to pass rules for the sector. A patchwork may protect some borrowers while leaving people in neighbouring states with different dispute rights for the same product.

The access question is therefore practical. Can a borrower identify the lender, pause repayment during a dispute, obtain a refund, and find affordable help before fees accumulate? A right that requires several applications, a lawyer, and months of waiting is worth more to a well-resourced claimant than to someone already choosing between rent and repayment.

## 2. Finance and financial inclusion: the oil shock will be felt first by households with the least room to adjust

Brent crude continued to rise as markets assessed the risk that the Strait of Hormuz could become subject to restricted navigation or toll demands. The AP reported that Asian shares gained after Wall Street’s rally, while energy prices kept moving higher. This is a classic unequal supply shock. Oil is priced globally, but households do not have equal ability to absorb higher transport, food, electricity, and delivery costs. Low-income households spend more of their budgets on necessities and have fewer ways to substitute away from them.

The IMF’s July World Economic Outlook update puts global headline inflation at 4.7% for 2026 and projects global growth at 3% this year and 3.4% in 2027. Those figures are broad aggregates. They can coexist with a sharper squeeze in countries that import energy or food, and with higher financing costs if central banks respond to second-round inflation. Singapore is exposed through imported energy and trade. The IMF’s latest Article IV assessment says the Middle East war has already caused a moderate increase in Singapore’s inflation, even though the country’s institutions and external buffers reduce the risk of a larger macroeconomic break.

The inclusion risk comes through credit. When income falls or necessities rise, a household may turn to credit cards, digital lenders, or informal lending circles. Each loan can appear manageable in isolation, but the combined repayment schedule may leave no buffer. A useful financial-inclusion measure should include the ability to withstand a price shock, understand total obligations, and obtain a hardship pause. More accounts and more approvals do not show that a household is safer.

## 3. Everyday microeconomics and platform power: interoperability is an attempt to make switching real

The European Commission issued binding specifications under the Digital Markets Act requiring Google to give competing AI assistants more equal access to Android functions and to share certain Google Search data with third-party search engines and AI chatbots. Android reaches about 60% of EU users, and the Commission says rival assistants have been restricted from functions that Google’s own Gemini can use. Users may technically be able to download another assistant, but a rival that cannot respond to voice commands, book a taxi, or act inside other apps is not an equal substitute.

This is a two-sided-market problem. Google benefits from controlling the operating system used by consumers and the data generated by searches, while developers need access to those users and inputs to build competing services. Default settings and restricted functionality create a status quo bias: consumers stay with the pre-installed option because the cost of testing an alternative is higher than the visible price. The Commission’s remedy attacks that hidden price by making competitors more useful before a user decides whether to switch.

There is a second pricing issue in the data-sharing requirement. The Commission specified an anonymisation process, an access procedure, and a formula for the price of shared data. That matters because a formal right to data can be neutralised by an excessive fee or a slow approval process. The success of the remedy will be measured by whether smaller search and AI firms can actually build better products, not by whether the legal specification exists on paper.

## 4. Tech, AI, and the new political economy: value is being allocated before productivity is shared

The ILO estimates that 22.9% of ASEAN employment, nearly 80 million workers, has more than minimal potential exposure to generative AI. Only 3.3%, or 11.7 million workers, are in the highest-exposure category, while about 67% of employment remains in occupations with no identified exposure. Singapore has the highest share of workers with more than minimal exposure at 42.2%, followed by the Philippines at 28.1%, Indonesia at 21.7%, Viet Nam at 20.8%, and Thailand at 20.6%. The ILO also finds that women are more than twice as likely as men to work in highly exposed occupations because of their concentration in clerical, administrative, and professional roles.

These numbers describe task exposure, not job losses. A worker can keep the same job while losing control over pace, evaluation, and the skills that command a wage premium. An employer may use AI to produce more with the same staff, or to reduce headcount, or to make each remaining worker handle more cases. The measured productivity effect may be positive in all three situations, but the effect on wages and job quality differs. That is why the ILO’s policy focus on skills, social protection, and support for smaller firms matters more than a simple count of jobs replaced.

The current market also shows how infrastructure scarcity can shape who receives the gains. Asian chip stocks rallied as AI-related export demand strengthened, while investors treated competition in AI infrastructure as a market opportunity. At the same time, the cost of chips, electricity, data, and cloud capacity determines which firms can deploy AI at scale. If those inputs remain concentrated, large platforms capture the surplus while smaller firms and workers pay for access. The $1.5 billion Anthropic settlement over pirated books used to train Claude adds a parallel ownership question: when data is an input into a valuable system, the original producers may need a legal mechanism to claim part of the surplus rather than rely on the platform’s terms.

The settlement is also a warning against treating data as free. A payment of roughly $3,000 per book to thousands of authors gives the courts a concrete way to price unauthorised inputs, but it does not answer every question about future training data, licensing, or the bargaining position of individual creators. The larger economic issue is who bears the cost of building the model and who receives the returns once the model is deployed.

Across the week, the pattern was control over adjustment. Energy producers and shipping chokepoints can transmit a global price shock; lenders can widen access without giving households a consolidated view of risk; courts can make a valid claim unaffordable through delay and costs; platforms can make alternatives nominally available but practically inferior; and AI companies can capture value from scarce compute and third-party data. Inclusive growth requires more than access at the front door. People also need room to absorb shocks, affordable ways to challenge decisions, and credible alternatives when an intermediary sets the terms.
