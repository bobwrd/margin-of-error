# 2026-07-23

The day’s market story is a bet on AI spending colliding with a fresh energy shock. Asian equities rose as Alphabet and Tesla kept spending heavily on AI infrastructure, while oil reached six-week highs as the Middle East conflict threatened shipping and supply. The distributional question is sharper than the market headline: firms with access to capital can buy compute and hedge energy costs, while households and workers absorb higher prices and weaker bargaining power.

## 1. Development, justice, and access: credit access without usable rights

Illinois became the second U.S. state to regulate buy-now-pay-later lenders after the federal administration abandoned a Biden-era rule. The change leaves consumers with different protections depending on where they live, even though the checkout interface and repayment model may look identical. Law.com reports that lenders now face a patchwork problem across the 50 states, but the consumer side is more direct: a borrower may have to work out which state rule applies before disputing a charge, seeking a refund, or challenging a repayment error.

That matters because BNPL shifts borrowing into the purchase flow. The small instalment is salient; the total exposure across several simultaneous purchases is not. A household that uses four separate providers may have no single view of its obligations, and a missed payment can still trigger account restrictions or collection activity. A legal right is weak when the person most affected cannot identify the lender, pause the payment, or obtain affordable advice before the balance grows.

This is a narrow but useful test for financial inclusion. Counting approval rates or new accounts says little about whether a borrower can survive a shock. A better test asks whether the product gives people clear total-cost information, a workable dispute channel, and a temporary hardship option. Federal withdrawal may reduce compliance costs for lenders, but it also transfers more of the research and enforcement burden to households and state regulators.

## 2. Everyday microeconomics: energy prices expose unequal room to adjust

Asian stocks gained on expectations that AI-related capital spending will support chipmakers, even as oil moved to six-week highs. Reuters reported that Alphabet raised its capital-expenditure plans and that earnings from Alphabet and Tesla showed no slowdown in AI infrastructure spending. Markets are treating the two developments as separate trades: chips and data centres on one side, energy and shipping risk on the other. For households, they arrive in the same budget.

Oil is priced globally, but adjustment capacity is not. A higher fuel bill can be absorbed by a large technology company, passed through to customers, or offset by a change in investment timing. A lower-income household has fewer substitutes for transport, electricity, and food delivery. The effect is also larger where wages are slow to adjust or where a household already uses credit to smooth ordinary expenses. A supply shock can therefore turn into a credit shock, with digital lenders and credit cards providing short-term relief at a cost that appears later.

The market’s attention to AI returns adds a second behavioural issue. Heavy spending can become an anchor for investors: once firms have committed billions to data centres, each new commitment may be read as evidence that the strategy must work. The relevant question is not whether spending is large, but who captures the productivity gain and who carries the downside if demand, energy availability, or model prices disappoint. The same sunk-cost logic can influence public policy when governments keep subsidising infrastructure to justify earlier commitments.

## 3. Platform power: the EU tries to make switching more than a menu option

The European Commission issued binding specifications under the Digital Markets Act requiring Google to give competing AI assistants more equal access to Android functions and to share certain Google Search data with third-party search engines and AI chatbots. Android reaches about 60% of EU users, and the Commission says rival assistants have been restricted from functions that Google’s own services can use. A user may technically download another assistant, but that service is not a close substitute if it cannot respond to voice commands or act inside other apps.

This is a two-sided-market problem. Google controls an operating system used by consumers and a large stream of search data that developers need to improve competing services. Defaults reduce the visible price of staying with the incumbent, while restricted functionality raises the hidden price of switching. The Commission’s remedy targets that gap by requiring access to functions that make a rival service useful at the moment of choice, rather than merely available in an app store.

The data rule also shows why formal access can fail in practice. The Commission specified anonymisation, an access procedure, and a formula for the price of shared data. If the price is too high or approval takes too long, the legal right will not produce a real competitor. The test is whether smaller firms can build and distribute credible alternatives, not whether a compliance document exists.

## 4. Tech, AI, and labour: exposure is not the same as bargaining power

The International Labour Organization estimates that 22.9% of ASEAN employment, nearly 80 million workers, is in occupations with more than minimal potential exposure to generative AI. Only 3.3%, or 11.7 million workers, are in the highest-exposure category, and around 67% of employment remains in occupations with no identified exposure. Singapore has the highest share of workers with more than minimal exposure at 42.2%, followed by the Philippines at 28.1%, Indonesia at 21.7%, Viet Nam at 20.8%, and Thailand at 20.6%. Women are more than twice as likely as men to work in highly exposed occupations because of their concentration in clerical, administrative, and professional roles.

These figures describe tasks, not a forecast of mass unemployment. A worker can keep the same job while losing control over pace, evaluation, and the skills that previously supported a wage premium. An employer may use AI to handle more cases with the same staff, cut headcount, or increase the workload assigned to each remaining worker. All three can raise measured output. Only the first clearly improves the worker’s position, and even then the gain depends on who owns the system and how its use is negotiated.

The Meta lawsuit reported by Reuters makes the evidence problem concrete. Former employees allege that discriminatory AI tools were used in layoff selection, while plaintiffs say proving how the tools were used is difficult because the relevant records sit with the employer. This is an access-to-justice issue as much as an AI issue. A worker cannot challenge an automated decision effectively if the employer controls the model, the input data, the scoring process, and the audit trail.

## Weekly pattern

Across the week, the same allocation problem appeared in different forms. Energy and shipping risks raise essential costs for people with little room to adjust; BNPL expands access while leaving borrowers to navigate fragmented remedies; platform rules determine whether a competitor is practically usable; and AI adoption can raise output without giving workers evidence, voice, or a share of the gain. The policy question is therefore not whether markets or technology are available. It is whether ordinary users can absorb shocks, switch providers, contest decisions, and retain enough bargaining power to benefit from the systems they help fund.
