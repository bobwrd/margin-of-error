# Daily Intelligence — 2026-05-31

This week’s clearest signal is that software keeps moving closer to the point where decisions happen, while the people affected by those decisions still carry the delay, the fee, or the mistake. That shows up in court queues, in housing costs, and in the push to let AI act inside finance and law. The surface looks more convenient. The risk still lands on the user.

## 1) Access to justice is getting slower where it matters most

The starkest legal story this week was the shutdown of San Francisco’s main immigration court after judges were fired, retired, or resigned, with roughly 117,000 cases shifted to Concord. That is not a small administrative change. It raises the effective cost of a hearing for people who already live with thin margins, long travel times, and little room to miss work. A system can keep the same filing rules and still become less accessible if the queue gets longer and the courthouse gets farther away.[^1]

The Meta case shows the other side of the same problem. The U.S. Supreme Court declined to hear Meta’s bid to dodge Vermont’s social-media-addiction lawsuit, leaving the state case alive over claims that Instagram was designed to hook young users.[^2] That matters because private lawsuits are one of the few tools left when public regulation is slow. The legal system is not just a place where disputes end. It is also where harmed users try to force design choices into the open, one complaint at a time.

## 2) Inclusion is now a housing and rates problem, not just a bank-account problem

If you want a clean snapshot of financial pressure, look at mortgage rates and housing costs. Reuters reported that the average U.S. mortgage rate hit a nine-month high, while AP reported thousands of people protesting rising housing costs in Madrid because they were being priced out despite the broader economy still growing.[^3][^4] That is what exclusion looks like in a richer, more connected economy: people may have accounts, cards, and apps, but they still get shut out when the price of shelter rises faster than wages.

The practical lesson is simple. Financial inclusion is not achieved when someone can open an account. It is closer to real when that person can survive rate shocks, rent spikes, and fee stacking without falling into debt or moving out. This is why the old bank-account metric misses so much. The pressure point is usually cash flow, not access in the abstract.

## 3) Platform power is showing up in labor organizing and lawsuit risk

Massachusetts ride-share drivers just certified the first statewide union of Uber and Lyft drivers in the U.S., covering nearly 70,000 independent contractors. That is a direct response to a market where the platform controls pricing, access to rides, and the rules of work, while drivers absorb the volatility.[^5] The interesting part is not the label “union.” It is the signal that workers are treating the app itself as the place where bargaining power lives or dies.

That same logic explains why platform firms keep ending up in court. If the business model depends on design tricks, recommendation loops, or hidden fees, then lawsuits become part of the cost of doing business. The longer companies keep treating the screen as if it were neutral, the more obvious it becomes that the screen is the market.

## 4) AI is moving from chat to delegated action

Robinhood’s move is the cleanest example. Reuters reported that it will let customers use AI agents to trade stocks and make credit-card purchases, even though a Deloitte survey found only 21% of leaders believed their organizations had a mature governance model for agentic AI.[^6] Once software is allowed to act, the question is no longer whether it can generate text. It is who can reverse an action, who can explain it, and who bears the loss if the model is wrong.

Law and banking are moving in the same direction. Reuters reported that Kirkland & Ellis plans to spend $500 million on its own AI platform, while Reuters also reported that OpenAI gave Japanese banks access to its latest model for cyber defense work.[^7][^9] Those are not random headlines. They show the same split in two different sectors: the valuable part of AI is moving into workflow control, data access, and exception handling. The Pope’s call for tougher AI regulation lands in the same place. He is arguing that the technology cannot be left to its developers’ profit logic alone.[^8]

The week’s pattern is consistent. The front end keeps getting easier to use, but the back end is still where power sits: in the backlog, the rate, the fee, the lawsuit, and the model owner. That is where the next fight is.
