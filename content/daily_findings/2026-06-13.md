# Daily Intelligence — 2026-06-13

The week’s biggest story is that inflation is still reshaping who gets squeezed and who gets protected. U.S. consumer prices rose 4.2% year on year in May, the hottest pace in three years, while the Fed still had no clean path back to 2% on the measure it actually targets.[^1][^2] That pressure shows up everywhere else in this brief: in emergency cash transfers, in court delays, in predatory credit, and in platform fees that quietly add another layer of cost.

## 1) Development, justice, and access

The clearest development story is not about growth, it is about survival. The World Bank warned that Cambodia’s vulnerable households need urgent, time-bound cash transfers after a fuel shock, border tension with Thailand, and a broader downturn pushed up transport and production costs.[^3] This is the part of macro that gets missed when policy talk stays abstract. A fuel shock does not just move CPI. It hits the cost of getting to work, moving food, and keeping informal businesses alive. For households with no buffer, the loss is immediate and blunt.

There is a similar pattern in digital finance. The CFPB said lenders can consider immigration status in some lending decisions, while still warning that removal risk can disrupt income and repayment capacity.[^4] That is a reminder that inclusion is often conditional. A person can have a bank account, a payment app, even a credit score, and still be treated as a risk bucket because their income path is unstable or their legal status is fragile. The system is not just measuring ability to pay. It is ranking who deserves a normal price.

The other edge of this problem is student debt. New discharge notices under borrower-defense settlements and the coming “new borrower” cutoff after July 1 show how much value sits in procedural timing.[^5] Miss the deadline, or get stuck in the wrong class of borrower, and the relief changes. That is what access looks like in practice: not a single yes or no, but a maze of dates, forms, and classifications that rewards people who can track the system closely.

## 2) Everyday microeconomics

The week’s pricing story is really about hidden add-ons. Consumer Reports said food and grocery delivery apps were charging 48% to 52% on top of the base cost of food and taxes, through service fees, delivery fees, perk subscriptions, and other layered charges.[^6] That is not just expensive. It changes how people decide. A user sees one food price, then a checkout bill that turns convenience into a premium product. The app looks simple; the economics are not.

Apple pushed the same logic in a cleaner form. It said the App Store facilitated more than $1.4 trillion in billings and sales in 2025, and it is now adding new bundle tools so developers can package subscriptions across apps.[^7][^8] The surface story is that this helps developers grow. The deeper story is that a gatekeeper can shape pricing, bundling, and discovery all at once. When one platform controls the channel, the bundle becomes a way to keep demand inside the wall while making the offer look cheaper than buying pieces separately.

That is why the platform fee fight matters beyond tech. Users often think they are choosing between merchants, but they are really choosing inside a system that controls ranking, payment, and exit. Once those pieces are bundled together, the firm gets room to collect a toll without having to raise the sticker price in a way that feels obvious.

## 3) Courts, legal labor, and the price of delay

Access to justice is getting thinner in places where the system depends on scarce labor. In California, legal nonprofits told the state Supreme Court that the shortage of court reporters has become a crisis, because litigants who cannot pay for a private reporter can lose the only verbatim record of what happened in court.[^9] In Hawaiʻi, the state Supreme Court created a task force to address a lawyer shortage that hits rural and neighbor-island communities hardest.[^10] The economics are straightforward: if the record is missing, or the lawyer is missing, the right exists mostly on paper.

The UK is showing the same strain through case backlogs. Employment lawyers there are pushing for tribunal reform because compulsory mediation and a multi-track system would be cheaper than letting the queue keep growing.[^11] That may sound procedural, but procedure is the product. The longer a low-income claimant waits, the more expensive the case becomes relative to the harm. Delay is not neutral. It favors the party with cash, patience, and an in-house legal team.

There is also a more basic labor shortage story here. Courts do not run on doctrine alone. They run on reporters, clerks, judges, and lawyers willing to work in places that do not pay enough to make the work easy. When those roles thin out, the market price of justice rises even if the rulebook does not change.

## 4) Tech, AI, and the new political economy

Platform power is getting regulated from two directions at once. The European Commission ordered Meta to restore free access to WhatsApp for rival AI assistants, saying the company had blocked third-party access in a growing market that competition law should protect before the damage hardens.[^12] At the same time, Reuters reported that a U.S. district court struck down the EPA’s termination of a $2.8 billion environmental justice grant program, reminding firms and agencies that the law still matters when money is tied to public rules.[^13] One case is about interfaces, the other is about grants, but both are about who gets to control the channel.

AI is also running into cost reality. CNBC reported that surging demand for compute is pushing up costs and creating a strain on AI start-up economics, with token rationing and cheaper model substitution starting to matter.[^14] That is the point where the labor story becomes less about grand replacement claims and more about task sorting. If compute is expensive, firms will not automate everything. They will automate the parts that fit the budget, then leave the rest to people under tighter oversight.

The market is already adjusting in small, visible ways. JioStar is tying sports content to Swiggy and ChatGPT-style interactions inside its app, while Apple is turning subscriptions into bundles and Meta is fighting over assistant access.[^8][^12] This is the same contest from different angles: who owns the user relationship, who owns the data path, and who gets the surplus when the interface becomes the market.

The pattern across the week is simple. The economy is still running on thin buffers, and the institutions that sit between people and cash are getting more important, not less. When prices rise, the user feels it in rent, food, and debt service. When courts are short-staffed, the user pays in time. When platforms own the checkout or the chat layer, the user pays in fees and lock-in. When AI scales, the bill first lands on compute, power, and control.
