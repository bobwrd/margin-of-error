# Daily Intelligence — 2026-06-03

The clearest pattern right now is that the economy keeps making the front door easier while the real cost moves deeper into the system. Courts are pushing more volume through thinner pipes, platforms are fighting over who can charge what toll, and AI is moving from drafting text into the control of workflows, data, and decisions. That is where the pressure is landing.

## 1) Development, justice, and access

The legal system is still sorting people by time, distance, and stamina. AP reported that San Francisco’s immigration court was effectively gutted after judges were fired, retired, or resigned, and that roughly 117,000 cases were shifted to Concord.[^1] Reuters also reported that the Justice Department is trying to cut a 3.7 million case immigration backlog by pushing out judges it sees as too slow.[^2] Those moves look administrative on paper. In practice, they raise the cost of appearing, especially for people who cannot absorb long travel, lost wages, or repeated hearings.

The more striking point is how often “access” still means only the ability to file, not the ability to finish. Bloomberg Law reported that AI-generated pro se filings are flooding dockets, with non-prisoner pro se civil cases rising from 11% of all civil cases five years ago to 16.8%.[^3] That can help someone get into court, but it also shifts cleanup work onto clerks, judges, and the other side. So the system gets more open at entry and more clogged at processing. That is a familiar trade-off in access policy: lower the front-end barrier and you can easily raise the back-end burden.

There is also a narrower financial-access story running through emerging markets. The Fintech Times reported that PayJoy has crossed 20 million customers and $3.5 billion in financed loans, using smartphones as collateral to extend credit to borrowers who are usually shut out of unsecured lending.[^4] That is inclusion, but it is inclusion on a tight leash. The model lowers underwriting risk by tying credit to a device people depend on for work, family, and payments. It expands access, yet it also makes the borrower’s phone part of the enforcement stack.

## 2) Everyday microeconomics

The cleanest consumer-price signal this week was still inflation. Reuters reported that U.S. consumer prices rose 3.8% in April, the biggest annual increase in three years, while gas and food were pulling spending decisions around.[^5] Reuters also said Goldman Sachs expects high oil prices to change consumer behavior in the second half of 2026 if inflation stays hot.[^6] That is the practical version of everyday microeconomics: people do not experience inflation as a neat index. They feel it through the next fill-up, the next grocery run, and the next trip they decide not to take.

The sharper pricing story is the fight over hidden fees and individualized prices. Maryland enacted a law banning personalized food prices based on consumer data, and New York City is also moving against surveillance pricing.[^7] In Europe, France’s top administrative court rejected Amazon’s challenge to minimum delivery fees on books.[^8] The common thread is that pricing is no longer just about supply and demand. It is about what a platform knows about you, how much friction it can hide until checkout, and whether regulators will let it segment demand by person instead of by product.

That matters because it changes who gets squeezed first. A household with flexibility can switch stores, shift timing, or wait out a surge. A household that needs the meal, the book, or the ride now pays the premium. The visible sticker is only part of the price. The rest sits in fees, timing rules, and data-driven discrimination.

## 3) Platform power, labor, and the app layer

Platform markets are still concentrating, and that changes the economics fast. Reuters reported that Uber confirmed an offer for Delivery Hero and later weighed a higher bid, while Delivery Hero shares hit an 18-month high after the news.[^9] That is not just an M&A story. In food delivery, scale can reduce some costs, but it also makes the market harder to exit and easier to toll. Once a handful of firms control demand and logistics, merchants and workers lose leverage.

The legal response is moving, but slowly. Reuters reported that the U.N. labour agency started final talks on binding standards for platform workers in ride-hailing, food delivery, and e-commerce.[^10] The same basic question keeps coming back: when an app sets pay, access to jobs, ratings, and routing, how independent is the contractor really? The platform can call itself a marketplace. The worker feels the control like an employer would impose it.

Apple’s contempt order in the Epic Games fight is part of the same story from another angle.[^11] App stores are not just distribution channels. They are fee-setting institutions with gatekeeping power. The fight over commissions, rankings, and payment rules is really a fight over rent extraction in the software layer. That is why the regulatory debate keeps circling back to competition law, labor law, and consumer protection at the same time.

## 4) Tech, AI, and the new political economy

AI is moving from novelty into ownership. Reuters reported that Anthropic filed confidentially for a U.S. IPO, with coverage pointing to a $965 billion valuation and a $47 billion annualized revenue run rate, while Microsoft used its developer conference to hint at AI-agent devices built to host specific tasks in industries like healthcare and retail.[^12][^13] The money is no longer just in model access. It is in the stack around the model: workflow design, device placement, data access, and the control rules that sit between output and action.

Law is being pulled into that same shift. Reuters reported that Kirkland & Ellis plans to spend $500 million on its own AI platform, and Bloomberg Law said AI-generated pro se litigation is already flooding dockets.[^14][^3] Both stories point to the same thing. The value is not in a generic chatbot. It is in the system that wraps it, checks it, and assigns responsibility when it fails. Firms with data, process control, and legal review will take the gains first. Everyone else gets the noise.

China’s new outbound-investment rules are another sign that AI and data are being treated as strategic assets, not just software inputs.[^15] That is the political economy underneath the hype: whoever controls the data, the workflow, and the export rules controls more than the model itself.

The week’s pattern is plain. Access is widening at the front door, but costs are moving into distance, fees, routing rules, and review layers. That is true in courts, in delivery markets, and in AI adoption. The surface gets smoother. The bargaining position does not.
