## Economics

### U.S.-China trade thaw leaves tariff risk in place
**What happened:** China said it would cut tariffs and improve farm-market access after the Trump-Xi summit in Beijing. Reuters also said the trade truce is still fragile, with pending U.S. investigations able to revive tariffs on goods entering the U.S.
**Analysis:**
1. **Shock type & market:** Positive trade-policy shock, but only partial. It hits the agricultural trade market and the broader bilateral trade regime. The shock is macro because it moves import prices, export revenue, and policy expectations at once.
2. **Model:** Basic open-economy trade model with tariffs and non-tariff barriers.
3. **Static equilibrium:** Lower trade wedges raise the quantity of U.S. farm goods sold into China and lower prices for Chinese buyers of those goods. U.S. farm producers gain producer surplus; China gives up some protection and tariff revenue.
4. **Dynamic path:** If the truce holds, firms will sign longer contracts, lower precautionary inventories, and invest more in shipping and processing capacity. If the truce breaks, the same firms will treat the market as unstable and keep a risk premium in prices and inventory decisions.
5. **Distribution & incidence:** U.S. farmers and exporters gain. Chinese feed mills and consumers gain from cheaper inputs. Domestic Chinese producers that compete with imports lose. Demand for farm commodities is inelastic in the short run, so volumes move less than prices at first, then more over time.
6. **Frictions & market failures:** The main problem is policy uncertainty. Non-tariff barriers, changing enforcement, and weak commitment devices keep the market from clearing smoothly. Each side can use trade rules as bargaining leverage, which raises transaction costs.
7. **Macro-financial / external transmission:** Better farm trade trims bilateral tension and can ease pressure on commodity prices. It also affects the external balance through higher U.S. export receipts and a small support to China’s food supply chain.
**Welfare & policy:** Welfare rises if the lower tariff wedge is durable. The cleanest policy is a written rollback with clear market-access rules, but that costs both sides bargaining power and leaves sensitive sectors more exposed.

### Oil supply shock keeps gasoline prices high
**What happened:** Reuters reported that the U.S. Treasury let a sanctions waiver lapse that had allowed some countries to buy Russian seaborne oil, after a temporary extension meant to ease shortages from the Iran conflict. Reuters also said gasoline has climbed past $4.50 a gallon and voters are blaming the president.
**Analysis:**
1. **Shock type & market:** Negative supply shock in the global oil market. It is persistent if the conflict and sanctions stay in place. The market is macro because it feeds directly into inflation and transport costs.
2. **Model:** Open-economy commodity model with short-run inelastic demand.
3. **Static equilibrium:** The supply curve shifts left. World oil prices rise, gasoline prices rise, and transport-sensitive sectors face a higher input bill. The first-round effect is a direct inflation impulse.
4. **Dynamic path:** The market adjusts through SPR releases, routing changes, higher drilling, fuel substitution, and eventual demand destruction. Those offsets are slow because refinery capacity, tanker routing, and field output do not move fast.
5. **Distribution & incidence:** Households lose, especially lower-income households that spend a bigger share of income on fuel and commuting. Airlines, trucking firms, and chemical producers also lose. Oil producers, shipping firms, and some fiscal authorities gain.
6. **Frictions & market failures:** Short-run fuel demand is hard to reduce, so the burden lands on prices. Sanctions, shipping bottlenecks, and geopolitical risk premia keep the shock from clearing quickly.
7. **Macro-financial / external transmission:** Higher oil prices lift headline inflation, push bond yields up, and can weaken risk assets. They also raise stress in emerging markets with fuel subsidies or dollar debt.
**Welfare & policy:** Welfare falls because the shock transfers income from consumers to producers and leaves deadweight loss from a higher price level. A temporary SPR release or targeted transfers can soften the hit, but each has a trade-off: SPR use weakens the supply cushion, while transfers add fiscal cost.

## Finance

### Iran stock market set to reopen after wartime suspension
**What happened:** Iran will reopen its stock market on Tuesday after suspending trading during the conflict with the U.S. and Israel.
**Analysis:**
1. **Shock type & market:** Market-reopening shock after a conflict shutdown. It is micro in the equity market, with some macro spillover through sentiment and wealth effects.
2. **Model:** Market microstructure with information asymmetry and thin liquidity.
3. **Static equilibrium:** Trading resumes, so price discovery comes back. Because the market has been shut and order books are likely thin, the first sessions can swing hard in either direction.
4. **Dynamic path:** If conflict risk fades, volatility should narrow and issuance can restart. If the conflict remains unresolved, trading stays jumpy and capital flight stays a live risk.
5. **Distribution & incidence:** Active traders and brokers regain business. Households with local equity exposure face mark-to-market swings. Firms gain a channel for financing, but only if investors trust the market.
6. **Frictions & market failures:** Thin liquidity, poor disclosure, and capital controls make prices noisy. In stressed markets, adverse selection is strong, so buyers demand a discount.
7. **Macro-financial / external transmission:** A functioning exchange can stabilize local wealth and sentiment, but global spillovers are limited. The bigger effect is domestic: it signals some return to normal market plumbing after the suspension.
**Welfare & policy:** Reopening is better than freezing the market, but only if authorities allow real price discovery. Better disclosure and market-making support would help, though both can expose the system to sharper short-run price moves.

### BOJ deputy governor calls for a broader future monetary system
**What happened:** Bank of Japan Deputy Governor Ryozo Himino said the future monetary system should not be limited to central bank digital currencies and stablecoins, and could include tokenized bank deposits and central bank reserves on blockchain rails.
**Analysis:**
1. **Shock type & market:** Policy-design shock for payments and settlement infrastructure. It is medium-run and mostly micro at first, with broader financial-market effects later.
2. **Model:** Two-sided network model for payment rails, with coordination frictions.
3. **Static equilibrium:** A wider menu of settlement tools raises optionality for banks and users. If the tools are interoperable, transaction costs can fall.
4. **Dynamic path:** Adoption depends on standards, legal finality, liquidity, and trust in reserve backing. If the design is messy, the market fragments instead of converging.
5. **Distribution & incidence:** Large banks and payment firms face more competition. Firms with cross-border flows gain from faster settlement. Smaller banks may lose deposits if tokenized products become easier to use.
6. **Frictions & market failures:** Coordination failure is the main one. Cyber risk, legal uncertainty, and incompatible systems can stop a useful payment tool from scaling.
7. **Macro-financial / external transmission:** Faster wholesale settlement can change cross-border capital flows and payment demand across currencies. Over time, that can alter the value of reserve assets and the economics of correspondent banking.
**Welfare & policy:** Welfare rises if payment costs fall and settlement is safer. The trade-off is tighter supervision, slower rollout, and the risk of creating a fragmented set of half-compatible systems.

## Law (Economics-Relevant)

### Kansas judge blocks law banning gender-transition treatments for minors
**What happened:** A Kansas judge temporarily blocked enforcement of a state law banning gender-transition treatments for minors, saying the parents who sued were likely to prevail on constitutional grounds.
**Analysis:**
1. **Shock type & market:** Regulatory shock in healthcare. It is persistent while litigation continues and affects a local care market.
2. **Model:** Standard IO / regulated healthcare market.
3. **Static equilibrium:** Providers keep serving patients under the current rules. Compliance risk falls relative to full enforcement, and access to care is preserved for now.
4. **Dynamic path:** If the injunction holds, clinics and insurers keep current billing and treatment pathways. If the law later survives, care shifts across state lines and the market re-sorts around travel, waiting lists, and higher search costs.
5. **Distribution & incidence:** Families seeking care gain access. Clinics keep demand. The state loses immediate enforcement power. The burden falls hardest on patients with fewer resources, because they are less able to travel.
6. **Frictions & market failures:** A patchwork of state rules creates legal uncertainty and travel costs. That raises the price of care and discourages providers from investing in specialized services.
7. **Macro-financial / external transmission:** The direct macro effect is small, but provider networks, insurer billing, and cross-state patient flows are affected.
**Welfare & policy:** Welfare is higher when medically indicated care remains available and the rules are clear. A narrower rule with explicit clinical guidance would lower uncertainty, but it also gives the state less room to set its own policy.
