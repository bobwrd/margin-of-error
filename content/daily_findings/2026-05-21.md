# Daily Intelligence — 2026-05-21

The week’s clearest signal is that access is being redrawn at the level of rails, rules, and interfaces. The Fed is weighing a narrower payment account for nonbanks, courts are forcing AI disclosure and fighting over public records, and platform pricing disputes keep moving from product design into law. The common thread is simple: the fee, the rule, or the filing form is where power sits.

## 1) Access to money is still about rails

The Fed proposed limited payment accounts that would let some firms use its payment rails without intraday credit, the discount window, or interest on reserves.[^1] That is inclusion with a hard boundary. Fintechs may get a door into the system, but not the safety net that makes bank access cheap in the first place. Trump’s order pushing the Fed to review fintech access to payment accounts points in the same direction: the fight is over who gets permission to stand close to the plumbing, not over who gets a nice app.[^2]

The bigger point is that “financial inclusion” is still mostly measured wrong. A phone wallet is not the same thing as credit history, underwriting, or a lender willing to take first loss. Finextra’s report on Thailand notes that millions without credit histories are still locked out, while a Cambodia piece argues that turning microfinance institutions into banks can weaken the inclusion mission instead of widening it.[^4][^5] Wells Fargo’s $110 million settlement, including a $100 million fund for low- and moderate-income mortgage assistance, says the same thing from the other side: access without fair terms is not real access.[^3]

## 2) Courts decide who can push back

Ohio justices questioned why their own court rules may restrict public access to records more than state law does.[^6] Bloomberg Law also reported that California’s high court has been short a justice for more than six months.[^7] These are procedural details, but they shape the price of justice. Delay, missing records, and unclear rules all help the side with more time and money.

The harder edge is how state power lands on people with weak bargaining power. Bloomberg Law reported that the Trump administration has issued 65,101 civil fines totaling more than $36 billion against immigrants and has started suing people to collect them.[^8] That is not just enforcement. It is a balance-sheet strategy: turn immigration status into a debt stream, then use court process to extract payment. In that setting, access to justice is less about rhetoric than about whether you can afford a lawyer, a filing, or a delay.

## 3) Platform pricing is becoming harder to hide

The Apple-Epic fight is still about the same thing it has always been about: who gets to take a cut at the point of sale. The Supreme Court declined to pause the contempt order against Apple, keeping pressure on the App Store fee model.[^9] France’s top administrative court also rejected Amazon’s challenge to minimum delivery fees on books.[^10] These cases look different, but both are about gatekeepers charging for access to a channel and then calling that structure “the market.”

The newest wrinkle is personalized pricing. MediaPost reported that 16 state attorneys general asked the FTC to crack down on food delivery platforms that set prices using consumer data.[^11] Reuters also reported that Shutterstock will pay $35 million to settle FTC charges over subscription plans and hard-to-cancel sign-ups.[^12] That is the same pattern in two forms: one is the visible surcharge, the other is the hidden trap. Consumers see a price; platforms see a way to sort users by willingness to pay and make exit harder.

## 4) AI is moving into law as infrastructure and liability

OpenAI’s governance fight is no longer abstract. Reuters reported that Sam Altman holds more than $2 billion in stakes in companies that did business with OpenAI, and that a California jury sided with OpenAI over Elon Musk’s lawsuit on the company’s for-profit conversion.[^13][^14] That does not settle the political question. It only says the company’s current structure has legal cover for now. The deeper issue is who gets the upside when model costs fall and distribution gets tighter.

Courts are also starting to write the rules around AI use, instead of waiting for Congress or the bar. Florida county courts are requiring disclosure when lawyers and self-represented litigants use generative tools in filings, and Reuters reported that Sullivan & Cromwell apologized after AI hallucinations appeared in a court filing.[^15][^16] That matters because legal work is one of the places where AI errors become cash costs fast. If the system pushes drafting speed down while pushing verification risk onto the filer, then the productivity gain is partly a transfer of work and risk, not a clean efficiency win.

The week’s pattern is the same across finance, consumer markets, and law: whoever owns the gate gets to set the terms. Payment rails, court records, app stores, and model governance are all becoming places where access is priced, filtered, or delayed. The fights look sector-specific, but the mechanism is one thing: control the choke point, and you control who can participate.
