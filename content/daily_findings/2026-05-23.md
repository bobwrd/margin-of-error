# Daily Intelligence — 2026-05-23

The clearest signal this week is that access is being priced through rules, not just through markets. Credit, pricing, court process, and AI deployment are all getting more selective at the same time, which means the people with the least room to absorb delay or fees feel the pressure first.

## 1) Credit is getting more visible, but not easier

The CFPB’s revised Section 1071 rule should make small-business lending easier to see, because lenders will have to report more data on who gets credit and on what terms. That matters because inclusion problems often hide in plain sight: you can have a loan market that looks active while still rationing credit by size, geography, or relationship. At the same time, the OCC’s move to stretch exam gaps for well-rated community banks to 78 months shows how supervision can get lighter for some firms even as reporting gets tighter for others. The result is a split system. More data at the top. Less touch at the edge.[^1][^2]

Outside the U.S., the same tension shows up in Cambodia’s microfinance debate. The argument for turning microfinance institutions into banks is about resilience, but the cost is that the sector can drift away from its inclusion role just when borrowers are already under strain. That is the basic trap in many credit systems: the more they are cleaned up for balance-sheet safety, the less willing they can be to take small, messy risks on low-income borrowers. Earned wage access products sit in the same lane. They are pitched as a cleaner answer to payday lending, yet they still depend on real-time income checks and can still carry high fees. The product changes. The cash squeeze does not.[^3][^4]

## 2) Pricing is moving from posted price to hidden price

Sixteen state attorneys general are pushing the FTC to curb personalized pricing in food delivery, which is a direct attack on the idea that the app should be able to charge one user more than another based on data it has collected. That is not a niche complaint. It is the core business logic of many platforms now: the user sees a price, but the platform is also pricing the user. Once that happens, the market stops being about a single posted rate and starts becoming a set of private negotiations the customer never sees.[^5]

The FTC’s warnings to 97 auto dealers about deceptive pricing point in the same direction. Regulators are no longer only chasing obvious fraud; they are moving after drip pricing, junk fees, and the little gaps between the advertised price and the real one. Law firms are in the same pressure field. A recent Law.com piece argued that AI-era legal pricing needs to shift toward defined scope, speed, senior judgment, and risk allocation rather than just hours billed. That is a useful test for every platform and service business right now. If AI lowers the cost of execution, the only way to keep old pricing is to hide the change in the fee stack.[^6][^7]

## 3) Courts decide who can wait

Ohio Supreme Court justices were skeptical of their own records-access rules, because the court’s superintendence rules can restrict public access more than state law does in other branches. That sounds technical, but it is really about power. If records are hard to get, the public has less ability to check what courts are doing, and weaker parties have less ability to build a case around what happened in the first place. Transparency is not a nice extra. It is part of the cost of using the system.[^8]

The same economics show up in employment law and student debt. UK employment lawyers are now calling for compulsory mediation and a multi-track tribunal system because the case backlog is getting heavy enough to change the value of a claim. And in the U.S., the Senate blocked a rollback of the Trump administration’s changes to Public Service Loan Forgiveness, so eligibility limits tied to an employer’s activities are still moving forward. That is a classic access problem: if the rule about who counts as eligible gets tighter, the benefit may still exist, but for fewer people. Process is doing the sorting.[^9][^10]

## 4) AI is moving from product to labor policy

The biggest AI story this week is not a model release. It is the size of the bet around it. Axios reported that Anthropic is on track for its first profitable quarter, with revenue expected to more than double to $10.9 billion in Q2, while its SpaceX compute deal runs at roughly $1.25 billion a month through 2029. That is infrastructure-scale spending. It tells you that AI is no longer just software. It is power, chips, data center space, and long-dated contracts.[^11]

Policy is starting to catch up, but unevenly. California’s governor ordered state agencies to prepare for AI-driven worker disruption by studying severance standards, unemployment insurance, transition support, worker ownership models, universal basic capital ideas, and training. The White House, by contrast, postponed its planned AI executive order after pushback from industry voices. On the labor side, Reuters reported that Epsilon India is doing much more work with roughly the same headcount, while a Microsoft-linked survey found that 58% of AI users say they are producing work they could not have produced a year ago, rising to 80% among the most advanced users. That is the split that matters: AI is raising output in some places, but it is also freezing headcount and shifting bargaining power. The gains are real. So is the squeeze.[^12][^13][^14][^15]

The week’s pattern is simple. Money, law, and AI are all moving toward systems that decide who enters, who waits, and who pays more for the same result. The fight is no longer about whether these systems matter. It is about who gets the surplus after the fee, the filing rule, or the model update has done its work.
