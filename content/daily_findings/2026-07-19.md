# Daily Intelligence — 2026-07-19

Date: 2026-07-19

The week’s central economic question is whether new technology and tighter market rules will widen access or simply move control to a different intermediary. Central banks are again balancing energy-driven inflation against employment, while AI policy is shifting from broad promises to questions about who owns the data, who captures the productivity gain, and who can seek redress when automated systems fail.

## 1. Development, justice, and access: a court can be open and still be hard to use

The UK judiciary said it had hired more than 2,300 magistrates since April 2025 as part of an effort to reduce court backlogs. More decision-makers can reduce waiting time, but the measure does not capture the claimant’s cost of getting a case ready. A low-income person still has to understand procedure, gather documents, attend hearings, and absorb lost wages or childcare costs. Those burdens make formal access unequal even when the courthouse itself is available.

North Carolina shows the other side of the bottleneck. The state budget effectively removed funding through the Interest on Lawyers’ Trust Accounts programme. Pisgah Legal Services said IOLTA had supplied $1.9 million in the previous year, around 15% of its $14 million budget. The affected cases include eviction, foreclosure, domestic violence, and protection orders. Because these are generally civil matters, people who cannot pay may have no automatic right to counsel. The economic result of a missed deadline can be a lost home or an unsafe household, not merely a slower legal process.

The policy distinction is useful: court capacity and claimant capacity are different inputs. Adding magistrates addresses the supply of decisions; legal aid helps people produce a usable claim or defence. Limited assistance by trained nonlawyers, under legal-aid supervision, could lower the entry cost for routine matters. It needs clear boundaries and escalation to a lawyer, because cheap assistance that leaves people unable to recognise a serious legal issue only shifts the risk.

## 2. Finance and financial inclusion: access to credit is not the same as resilience

The Federal Reserve’s rate debate has become more sensitive to the conflict between inflation and employment. Vice Chair Philip Jefferson said rates might need to rise if inflation does not improve soon, while the Fed’s current stance is intended to support the labour market as tariff and energy effects pass through. Canada’s central bank held its policy rate at 2.25% and raised its 2026 inflation forecast to 2.5% from 2.3%. These decisions show how an energy shock travels unevenly: a central bank reacts to the aggregate price index, while lower-income households feel the effect through fuel, transport, and food budgets.

The distributional problem is sharper in consumer credit. Reuters reported that a 27-year-old Chinese worker had accumulated about 140,000 yuan, roughly $20,685, across credit cards, online borrowing, and a car loan after a pay cut and loss of a fuel allowance. China Merchants Bank’s personal-loan non-performing-loan ratio reached 1.14% in the first quarter, up 0.13 percentage point from a year earlier. The relevant measure of inclusion is therefore not whether a person can receive instant approval. It is whether emergency liquidity remains repayable after income falls, and whether the borrower can see the total exposure across several lenders.

This is where digital credit can reproduce the old problem of informal debt at greater speed. Each app may assess only its own repayment stream, while the borrower experiences one combined monthly obligation. A stronger consumer-protection test would require clear total-cost disclosure, checks for simultaneous borrowing, and a workable pause or restructuring route after a documented income shock. Without those features, access may increase the number of people connected to credit while reducing their room to absorb a setback.

## 3. Everyday microeconomics and platform power: the fee is only one part of the price

A California federal judge said there was “no time to waste” in monitoring an injunction governing Google’s Android app-store practices after the Epic antitrust case. The court’s demand for monthly reports matters because a formal remedy is not the same as a competitive market. Developers may be allowed to use alternative payment routes, yet Google can still influence discovery, default placement, security approval, updates, and the user relationship. A rival payment option that is hard to find will not exert much pressure on the incumbent.

The same logic applies to the proposed $14.8 billion Uber acquisition of Delivery Hero. A larger delivery network may reduce matching and logistics costs, but restaurants and couriers could become more dependent on one platform’s ranking system, advertising tools, data, and access to demand. The consumer-facing fee is only one measure of power. Switching costs, the ability to contact customers outside the platform, and control over visibility can matter more than a small change in commission.

Prediction markets add a related legal problem. Kalshi told a Michigan judge that it would comply with an order to unwind certain residents’ sports trades while arguing that it could not follow a Commodity Futures Trading Commission directive to reinstate the contracts. The dispute shows what happens when a new product crosses old regulatory boundaries. Participants face uncertainty not only about price, but about whether a contract will remain valid when two authorities claim different powers. In these markets, legal classification becomes part of the product’s economic risk.

## 4. Tech, AI, and the new political economy: productivity depends on bargaining power

More than 200 researchers and economists, including 15 Nobel laureates and researchers from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google, called for policies and institutions to address AI’s economic effects, including the risk of large-scale job displacement. The call matters less as a forecast than as a warning about measurement. A firm can report higher output per worker while workers lose hours, bargaining power, or access to the tasks that build experience. Productivity is a production ratio; it does not say who receives the resulting income.

The current market is also being priced through infrastructure concentration. Asian technology companies raised a reported $84 billion in the year to July 10, more than triple the amount in the same period of 2025, as investors pursued firms tied to the AI supply chain. SoftBank’s Masayoshi Son said AI could require $5 trillion of annual investment by 2040. Those figures describe capital demand, not proof that the spending will produce broad wage gains. Returns will depend on whether AI tools remain concentrated in a few cloud, chip, and platform firms or diffuse to smaller businesses and workers who can use them without surrendering most of the surplus.

China’s World Artificial Intelligence Conference gave the political side of the same issue a public form. Xi Jinping called for a “people-centred” approach to AI and presented access and governance as part of China’s international role. The practical test is whether access includes usable rights: data portability, notice when automated decisions affect a person, independent redress, and protection against exclusion. Policy language about inclusion has little economic force if users cannot move their data, challenge a decision, or share in the gains from systems trained on their work.

Across the week, the same pattern appears in different institutions. Courts can add decision-makers while legal-aid funding falls; lenders can widen approval while household repayment capacity shrinks; platforms can lower a fee while keeping control of discovery and customer data; and AI can raise measured output while leaving workers with less bargaining power. The common policy question is not whether access exists on paper. It is who can wait, compare, appeal, export, or switch when the intermediary changes the price, product, or rules.
