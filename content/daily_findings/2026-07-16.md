# Daily Intelligence — 2026-07-16

Date: 2026-07-16

The most useful signal today is that the AI investment cycle is becoming a macroeconomic and distributional issue, not only a technology story. TSMC reported a 77% jump in second-quarter profit as demand for advanced AI chips stayed strong, yet Asian shares fell sharply as investors questioned how much of the boom is already priced in. At the same time, China’s rising consumer defaults and new U.S. banking guidance aimed at undocumented workers show how access to credit can tighten precisely when households have fewer ways to absorb a shock.

## 1. AI investment is lifting output, prices, and concentration at the same time

TSMC’s record quarter is evidence of real demand rather than a purely speculative narrative. The company’s profit rose 77% year on year, and its advanced 3-nanometre, 2-nanometre, and chip-packaging capacity remains tied to AI infrastructure. But the market response was less straightforward: Taiwan’s chip story did not prevent a broader sell-off in Asian technology shares, and South Korea’s KOSPI fell 6.2% as Samsung and SK Hynix weakened. Investors are separating the strength of end-demand from the price paid for exposure to it.

That distinction matters for the wider economy. The firms controlling scarce compute, memory, packaging, and electricity infrastructure can capture revenue before productivity gains reach ordinary businesses or workers. Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh said AI investment may push prices higher in the near term, while Fed minutes showed that many officials expect demand for AI infrastructure to put upward pressure on technology products and electricity. A one-time price increase is not automatically persistent inflation, but it is still a transfer from users of scarce inputs to their owners. The policy question is therefore both monetary and distributional: who gets the productivity gain, and who pays during the build-out?

## 2. Consumer credit is becoming a measure of economic insecurity

Reuters reported a sharp rise in Chinese consumer defaults that complicates Beijing’s effort to revive household spending. One 27-year-old telecoms maintenance worker was left with about 140,000 yuan, or roughly $20,685, in debts across credit cards, online borrowing, and a car loan after a pay cut and the loss of a fuel allowance. China Merchants Bank’s personal-loan non-performing-loan ratio reached 1.14% in the first quarter, up 0.13 percentage point from a year earlier.

The mechanism is familiar: households borrow to smooth consumption, then an income shock turns several small obligations into a large fixed burden. Digital borrowing can make credit look accessible while hiding the combined cost across lenders and products. Once repayment problems begin, the borrower loses flexibility, and the macroeconomic result is weaker consumption rather than stronger demand. The policy lesson is that account ownership or app access is a weak measure of financial inclusion. The better test is whether households can borrow at a price they can survive when wages fall.

U.S. bank regulators also warned lenders to be cautious about lending to people who are not legally authorized to work, citing income reliability and deportation risk. The guidance is nonbinding, but it can still change bank behaviour because compliance teams often respond more broadly than the text requires. For workers who already rely on informal borrowing, remittances, or high-cost digital credit, a wider withdrawal of formal lenders would raise the cost of ordinary shocks. Financial regulation can reduce bank risk while increasing exclusion if it treats legal status as a proxy for repayment capacity instead of assessing actual income and safeguards.

## 3. The Google Play case shows why platform remedies must address network effects

Google and Epic withdrew a proposed settlement, leaving Google bound by the fuller antitrust remedies imposed by Judge James Donato. Google says it is prepared to distribute third-party app stores through Google Play from July 22. The court’s approach recognises the central economic problem: a rival store cannot compete merely by existing if users have to find and sideload it separately while developers remain dependent on the dominant store’s audience.

The original dispute began with Epic’s objection to the roughly 30% cut charged by Apple and Google on in-app purchases. The remedies include lower fees, access to Google Play’s app catalogue, and placement for rival stores. Google can charge approved third-party stores $5,000 per year for security and compliance review, and stores can be removed if more than 1% of attempted installs appear to be malware or unwanted software. Those conditions may protect users, but they also leave Google in a gatekeeping role. The practical test is whether a developer can reach customers without paying the incumbent or accepting terms set by the incumbent.

This is a direct example of two-sided market power. Developers need users, users need a reliable catalogue, and the platform controls the connection between them. A lower fee is useful, but it may not change the market if discovery, default placement, security approval, and catalogue access remain concentrated. Antitrust enforcement works better when it targets the mechanism that protects the incumbent, not only the percentage of revenue taken at checkout.

## 4. AI governance is moving toward control of infrastructure and work

China’s President Xi Jinping is expected to set out a vision for China’s role in global AI governance at the World Artificial Intelligence Conference, while Huawei presents a large domestic AI-computing cluster. Nvidia separately announced partnerships with Japanese robotics companies including Fanuc and Yaskawa. These developments point to a broader contest over physical AI: the firms and states that control chips, industrial equipment, data centres, and standards can influence which systems are adopted and where the surplus goes.

The labour effects are less settled than the investment story. Research covered by CNBC says AI may push some older workers out of jobs while helping others work longer by making their roles more efficient. The difference depends on task design, training, health, bargaining power, and whether productivity gains are shared through pay or used to reduce headcount. Measuring only output per worker misses the adjustment cost. A system can raise measured productivity while transferring risk to workers who have less ability to change occupations late in their careers.

The regulatory response is beginning to focus on institutional capacity. The Federal Reserve has created task forces on its inflation framework, AI’s effects on jobs and productivity, and the quality of economic statistics. Australia is establishing an AI office to coordinate government policy. These offices may reduce fragmented rules, but coordination is not a remedy by itself. Workers and affected consumers still need clear explanations, usable appeal routes, and a way to challenge automated decisions. Without those rights, AI governance can become a process for approving deployment rather than a system for distributing its gains and losses fairly.

The week’s pattern across development, everyday markets, and technology is concentration at the points where people depend on systems they cannot easily replace. Credit apps can smooth consumption and then amplify a pay shock; app stores can connect millions of users and still make developers accept a private toll; AI infrastructure can raise output while making chips, electricity, data, and employment more dependent on a small group of firms. The common measure is not access in the formal sense. It is bargaining power when a household, worker, or smaller business needs the system to keep functioning.
