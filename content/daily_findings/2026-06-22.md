# Daily Intelligence — 2026-06-22

The same pattern keeps showing up across finance, law, and tech: access is still open on paper, but only after you clear more checks, more fees, and more delay. This week’s stories point to a market where credit, court time, and platform access are all being rationed through systems that look neutral from far away and selective up close.

## 1) Development, justice, and access

Brazil’s subsidized motorcycle credit program for app-based delivery drivers is inclusion with a narrow door. The state is offering annual rates of 11.5% for women and 12.5% for men, but only to drivers who have been on a platform for at least six months and completed at least 100 trips or deliveries.[^1] That is useful if you already live inside platform work. It does less for the worker who needs the bike before the platform history exists. Credit is being tied to legibility.

Spain’s migrant legalisation drive tells the same story from the other side of the border. The government has received about 900,000 applications, roughly double what it expected.[^2] Once demand gets that high, the legal question turns into a queue question, then a paperwork question, then a cost question. Time, transport, translation, and document help all matter. People with those things move faster; people without them pay more in delay.

Court economics shows up in the cost of defending yourself, too. Fed Governor Lisa Cook has disclosed almost $1.2 million in legal services payments as her case over firing by Donald Trump heads toward the Supreme Court.[^3] That is an elite example, but it still says something plain: even before a court decides anything, the price of staying in the fight can be huge. For ordinary people, the same logic shows up as missed work, legal aid gaps, and months lost to process.

## 2) Everyday microeconomics

The app-store fight in Brazil is a clean example of how a platform can charge for the route itself. After a deal with the antitrust watchdog, Apple said developers in Brazil can use alternative marketplaces and outside payment systems.[^4] That matters because the store is not just a store. It is a toll road, and toll roads shape what sellers can keep. The visible price on the screen is only part of the cost.

ING’s new subscription-based banking model points in the same direction.[^5] Banks are moving from one-off fees and margin spread toward recurring charges that are easier to defend and harder to ignore. The tactic is simple: make the revenue steady, make the service bundle look convenient, and let the customer sort through the trade-offs later. That is how pricing power works when the market is crowded but switching is still annoying.

The broader pattern is less about any single price and more about the way price gets hidden. Default settings, tiered bundles, and recurring subscriptions shift the burden of comparison onto the user. Most people do not audit every fee line by line. They take the path that is easiest to follow. Once a platform owns that path, it can move the cost without changing the headline experience much at all.

## 3) Tech, AI, and the new political economy

AI is turning into a financing story as much as a software story. Morgan Stanley expects AI-related debt issuance to top nearly $570 billion in 2026, while hyperscalers are still expected to spend about $700 billion this year.[^6] OpenAI reportedly burned $3.7 billion in the first quarter of 2026 on $5.7 billion of revenue.[^7] That says the buildout is being carried by capital markets and very large spend commitments. The best model is not enough on its own. You also need the balance sheet to survive the race.

That is why the control points are moving closer to workflow and infrastructure. Salesforce agreed to buy Fin for about $3.6 billion, a bet on an AI agent layer that sits near the customer and the task itself.[^8] Jeff Bezos also said AI would lead to labour shortages rather than human replacement.[^9] Maybe in some narrow cases. But the near-term effect looks more like task reshuffling, with more control over the work sitting higher up the chain and the gains collected by firms that can fund the build.

The legal pressure is landing on the physical footprint. The Trump administration backed xAI in a lawsuit brought by the NAACP over emissions tied to data centers powering the company’s AI system.[^10] At the same time, Eurocommerce is asking the EU to exempt AI-generated ads from new transparency rules.[^11] That is the shape of the next fight: not just what the model says, but what it costs to run, who carries the externalities, and how much disclosure the firms behind it will accept.

The week’s pattern is blunt. Credit is being limited by proof. Legal rights are being limited by time and money. Platform markets are charging for access, not just goods. AI is being financed with debt and defended through regulation. The door is still there, but more of the economy now runs through gatekeeping.
