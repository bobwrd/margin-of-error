# Daily Intelligence — 2026-05-26

The week’s clearest signal is that access is getting split into layers. A bank app, a court portal, or a marketplace storefront can look open while the real terms sit deeper in settlement rules, backlog queues, or fee schedules. The fight is over who gets into the pipe, who pays the toll, and who has to wait.

## 1) Financial inclusion is moving from accounts to rails

The Federal Reserve’s proposal for a limited “payment account” is a good example of how finance is changing. It would let some eligible nonbanks clear and settle payments directly, but without intraday credit, the discount window, or interest on balances. That is broader reach, but not full membership in the banking system. The line is now between who can touch the rail and who still gets the backstops that make liquidity cheap when stress hits.[^1]

That split shows up in the private sector too. Trump’s order to review fintech access to payment accounts pushes the same direction, and JPMorgan’s Chase launch in Germany shows incumbents are building the same kind of digital front end themselves.[^2][^3] The user-facing layer can look clean while control over routing, settlement, and funding stays concentrated. For low-income users, migrants, and small firms, that matters more than app design. A nicer interface does not help much if the hidden rules still decide who gets paid first, who gets held up, and who pays for speed.

The development side of the story is similar. At the France-Africa summit, Macron’s financial push was described as too thin for what debt-strapped economies actually need, with analysts saying debt relief and payment pauses would have mattered more than another risk-sharing promise.[^4] That is the same problem in a different form: access without relief still leaves the weak side holding the risk. Whether it is a fintech customer or a sovereign borrower, the question is not just whether money can move. It is who absorbs the loss when things go wrong.

## 2) Courts are rationing time, and time is money

Employment lawyers in the UK are calling for radical tribunal reform because the backlog keeps growing. Their fix is a compulsory mediation layer and a multi-track system based on claim value. That is not just procedural tinkering. It is an admission that one-size-fits-all court design makes small claims and low-income claimants wait too long for a system that already asks too much of them.[^5] A separate call from barristers to release a delayed £34 million legal aid boost points to the same pressure: if specialist advocates are scarce and funding is late, the people with the least slack are the first to fall out of the process.[^6]

The U.S. immigration courts show the same arithmetic at bigger scale. The Justice Department is trying to push out judges it sees as too slow while the system sits on a 3.7 million case backlog.[^7] That is a blunt reminder that court economics are not abstract. Delay is a cost, and cost is not shared evenly. People with money can buy motion practice, experts, and patience. People without it absorb the damage first, then often settle or drop out.

## 3) Platform pricing keeps getting dragged into court

The Apple-Epic fight is still the cleanest case of platform pricing as a toll booth. The Supreme Court declined to pause the contempt order that found Apple in violation of court-mandated changes to App Store fees, and Epic said Fortnite was back in app stores worldwide.[^8] The legal fight is about more than one app. It is about whether a platform can set the payment rules, keep a big commission, and block outside routing while claiming it is just offering distribution. That model works until judges decide the fee is the product.

France and India are pushing in the same direction from different angles. France’s top court rejected Amazon’s challenge to minimum delivery fees on books, and an Indian court told Apple to fully cooperate in an antitrust case tied to the iPhone apps market.[^9][^10] These cases land on the same point: digital platforms can make a fee feel small at checkout, then stack it across delivery, access, and settlement. Once the platform owns the choke point, the price is never just the sticker price.

## 4) AI is reshaping work before it shows up as growth

Japan’s three largest banks are expected to gain access to Anthropic’s Mythos model, which is a sign that AI is moving deeper into regulated finance.[^11] At the same time, Reuters reported that rising U.S. law-firm expenses, including AI investment, were offsetting strong demand and rate hikes in the first quarter. In other words, the first effect of AI is often not a clean productivity jump. It is a cost shift. Firms spend on tools, oversight, and process changes before they know whether the gains will stick.[^12]

That is why labor and regulation are starting to move. Organized labor is pressing for a bigger say in how employers deploy workplace AI, and the UK is probing Microsoft’s business software dominance with an eye on how AI competitors can plug into the stack.[^13][^14] The real issue is control over workflow, data, and distribution. If AI only helps the firms that already own those layers, then “productivity” is mostly a way to widen the gap between owners and workers. If it is going to help workers too, the gains have to show up in wages, bargaining power, or lower barriers to entry. Right now, that part is still up for grabs.

The pattern across the week is plain. Finance is widening access at the edge while keeping the safety net tight. Courts are moving faster for institutions than for ordinary claimants. Platforms are getting pushed on fees because they turned convenience into a rent stack. AI sits on top of all of it and will mostly reward whoever already controls the rails, the records, and the workflow.
