# Daily Intelligence — 2026-05-25

The week’s clearest signal is that access is getting split into layers. You can still reach an app, a bank interface, or a court filing portal, but the terms behind those doors are narrowing: who gets routed, who pays fees, who waits, and who can afford help. That shows up in finance, courts, pricing, and AI at the same time.

## 1) Finance is moving from account access to rail access

The Federal Reserve’s proposal for limited payment accounts for fintechs is a good example of where finance is heading. The idea would let nonbanks move money across Fed rails, but without intraday credit, a discount window, or interest on reserves.[^1] That is a real expansion of reach, but it is also a hard line around the privileges that still belong to chartered banks. In other words, the system can let more firms touch the pipe while keeping the pipe’s backstops fenced off.

That distinction matters because the fight is no longer just about whether people have an account. Trump’s order asking the Fed to review fintech access to payment accounts pushes in the same direction, and JPMorgan’s Chase launch in Germany shows incumbents are building the same kind of digital front end themselves.[^2][^3] The practical question is who controls the layer behind the app. If the user can see a clean interface but not the rules for settlement, routing, or liquidity, inclusion is shallow.

## 2) Courts still sort people by patience and paperwork

Reuters’ report on Purdue’s opioid settlement is a blunt case study in access to justice. The settlement set aside about $865 million for harmed individuals out of more than $57 billion in total payouts, yet many victims were still being pushed down by documentation demands years after the deadline for filing claims had passed.[^4] That is not just a legal headache. It is a cost barrier. If proving harm takes records, time, and legal help, then the people with the least room to wait are the first to lose out.

The same logic shows up in criminal and public-records cases. The Philippine Supreme Court rejected a bid by Senator Ronald dela Rosa to block arrest under an ICC warrant, and the country’s National Bureau of Investigation said it would act “without delay.”[^5] In Ohio, justices were openly uneasy about court rules that can restrict records access more tightly than other branches of government.[^6] Different systems, same shape: process is not neutral. Whoever controls timing and access controls a large part of the outcome.

## 3) Platform pricing keeps shifting the rent

The Epic-Apple fight is still the cleanest example of platform power as fee setting. Fortnite returned to global App Stores after the U.S. Supreme Court declined to pause the contempt order against Apple, and the dispute still turns on the commission Apple can charge and the limits on external payments.[^7] This is not only a legal story. It is a pricing story. The platform sits in the middle, sets the toll, and decides how much of each transaction it keeps.

France and India are pushing back from different angles. France’s top court rejected Amazon’s challenge to minimum delivery fees on books, while an Indian court told Apple to cooperate in an antitrust case tied to the iPhone apps market.[^8][^9] The common thread is that digital platforms can turn convenience into a fee stack. The customer sees one price. The merchant, publisher, or developer sees another once the platform takes its cut.

## 4) AI is changing hiring, but the gains are uneven

In India, companies are shifting toward contract and outsourced hiring as AI uncertainty changes workforce planning, according to TeamLease.[^10] At the same time, Epsilon India says it is doing much more work with roughly the same headcount.[^11] That is the pattern worth watching. AI is not only replacing tasks; it is also freezing hiring, changing job mix, and giving managers a way to ask for more output without adding people.

The same split is visible in legal and financial services. Anthropic has expanded Claude’s tools for law firms and linked them with Thomson Reuters’ CoCounsel, while Japan’s three largest banks are expected to gain access to Anthropic’s Mythos model.[^12][^13] But small firms are not in the same position as global banks or large legal teams. A recent launch of OpenSpecter, a free self-hosted legal AI platform, was framed explicitly as a response to enterprise pricing that locks smaller firms out.[^14] So the AI story is not just about productivity. It is also about who can pay for the tools, who gets the first gain, and who gets pushed into thinner margins.

The pattern across the week is simple. Finance is tightening the rails, courts are sorting by patience, and platforms are turning convenience into a fee ladder. AI sits on top of that system and can widen the gap if it raises output without changing who controls the rails, the records, or the fees.
