# Daily Intelligence — 2026-06-12

The main story this week is that AI has turned into a financing problem. SpaceX raised $75 billion in the largest IPO ever, and Morgan Stanley now sees AI-related debt issuance reaching nearly $570 billion in 2026, with the big hyperscalers expected to spend about $700 billion this year.[^1][^2] That kind of money changes more than the tech market. It changes who gets access to capital, who gets priced out, and who ends up paying for the build-out.

## 1) Development, justice, and access

Digital access is getting wider, but the terms are getting stricter. ING launched a subscription-based banking model in the Netherlands and said it plans to roll it out across its markets by mid-2027.[^3] JPMorgan also opened its Chase digital retail bank in Germany.[^4] Put those together with Bybit’s plan to give retail customers tokenized access to SpaceX’s IPO, and the pattern is clear: more people can get through the door, but the door now comes with tiers, fees, and product design that push users into managed paths.[^5]

That is not the same as broad inclusion. A basic bank account used to be about getting into the system. Now access is often sold as a bundle, a subscription, or a tokenized slice of something bigger. That helps platforms widen reach, but it also shifts more risk and more cost onto the user. The result is a thinner version of inclusion: enough to transact, not always enough to absorb a shock.

## 2) Everyday microeconomics

Pricing power is showing up in delivery, resale, and platform checkout. Uber has made a takeover offer for Delivery Hero that values the company at 33 euros per share, while the UK competition watchdog opened a probe into eBay’s $1.2 billion purchase of Depop.[^6][^9] Amazon also lost its challenge to French minimum delivery fees on books, which matters because delivery charges are now part of the price, not just a side cost.[^7] Even Walmart’s fast-delivery push tells the same story. It is adding Subway meals to its 30-minute offering and plans to expand that service to about 1,400 stores by late summer.[^8]

The small details here matter more than the headlines. Delivery fees, subscription layers, and checkout design are where firms can shape what people buy without touching the sticker price on the front page. That is why these markets feel sticky. The user may think they are choosing between merchants, but the real choice is often between systems that control discovery, payment, and speed.

Inflation keeps that pressure alive. U.S. consumer prices rose at the fastest pace in three years in May, with Reuters’ poll showing economists expecting 4.2% year-on-year CPI and a 0.5% monthly rise.[^10] A separate Reuters poll said most economists now expect the Fed to hold rates through the rest of 2026.[^11] For households, that is not an abstract macro call. It shows up as a worse mortgage quote, a more expensive delivery route, and less room to ignore small price changes.

## 3) Tech, AI, and the new political economy

AI is being financed like a utility build-out, not a software launch. Morgan Stanley’s forecast of nearly $570 billion in AI-related debt issuance this year sits next to the $700 billion hyperscaler capex estimate and SpaceX’s record IPO.[^1][^2] That is a large pile of money for chips, land, power, cooling, and credit. It also means the people who own the infrastructure and the distribution layer will take the first cut of the gains.

The labor side is already showing strain. A Reuters/Ipsos poll found that half of Americans fear AI could put someone in their household out of work, while 73% said they were worried about increased AI use.[^12] At the same time, U.S. cyber officials shortened the fix window for the most serious network flaws to three days, partly because hackers are using AI.[^13] So the same technology is now tied to job fear, faster attack cycles, and a push for tighter rules. That is why the policy fight is moving away from abstract talk and toward control of compute, security, and the work that sits between them.

## 4) Courts, enforcement, and the cost of delay

The legal system is tightening who can sue and who has to wait. The U.S. Supreme Court sided with a group of BlackRock-affiliated funds in a case that blocks certain private suits under the Investment Company Act.[^14] At the same time, the Justice Department moved to strip citizenship from 17 naturalized people.[^15] Those are very different cases, but they point in the same direction. The state is making more decisions that force people to spend time and money just to stay in the game.

The Senate’s move to block debate on FISA reauthorization adds the same pressure from another angle.[^16] When the process stalls, the cost is not evenly shared. People with money can hire counsel, wait, and keep filing. People without it eat the delay. That is the real access-to-justice problem in economic terms. The law does not just decide outcomes. It decides who can afford the process.

The week’s pattern is simple. Access is expanding at the surface, but the real terms are getting tighter underneath. Finance is moving toward subscriptions, tokenization, and higher fees. Platforms are turning delivery and checkout into toll roads. AI is pulling in debt, capex, and labor anxiety. Courts are asking more of people who already have the least room to wait.
