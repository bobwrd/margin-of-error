# Daily Intelligence — 2026-06-02

The cleanest story this week is that systems are still getting more convenient at the edge, while the real costs keep moving into queues, fees, and review layers. Courts are clearing cases more aggressively, platforms are tightening their grip on labor, and AI is shifting from text generation into capital allocation and workflow control. The result is a more usable front end and a harder back end.

## 1) Development, justice, and access

The biggest access-to-justice number in the mix is the U.S. immigration backlog. The Justice Department said it wants to weed out judges it sees as too slow, as the system tries to cut down a backlog of 3.7 million cases.[^1] AP separately reported that San Francisco’s immigration court was effectively gutted after judges were fired, retired, or resigned, and that roughly 117,000 cases were pushed to Concord.[^2] That is a simple economics problem dressed up as procedure: if the system moves the hearing farther away, the cost of showing up rises immediately. Time, transit, and missed work become part of the filing burden.

What matters here is not only delay. It is how delay changes bargaining power. A person with money can keep a case alive across months of waiting, repeated appearances, and legal handoffs. A person without it is more likely to miss deadlines, accept weak deals, or give up. That is why access to justice is often misread when people focus only on filing fees or the right to appear. The real filter is the accumulation of small frictions that hit low-income people first.

There is also a wider political point. Courts can look neutral while still sorting people by stamina. The immigration docket shows that clearly, but the same logic appears in civil and administrative systems everywhere. If case management is treated as a purely technical task, the system starts rewarding the side that can wait longer. That is not legal neutrality. It is a cost advantage.

## 2) Everyday microeconomics

Households are still feeling the price shock in ways that are easy to miss if you only look at aggregate inflation. AP reported that the key inflation gauge rose to 3.8% in April, the highest in three years, while gas prices climbed to about $4.49 a gallon and consumers were changing their spending habits.[^3] AP also reported that higher summer flight and hotel prices are splitting travel into haves and have-nots, with American Airlines saying demand has taken a K-shaped pattern.[^4] That is the microeconomics story in one line: the sticker price is only the first filter. Flexibility, timing, and the ability to absorb a shock matter just as much.

This is where price discrimination stops being abstract. Airlines already segment by time, route, and cancellation rules. Hotels do the same with room class and booking windows. When fuel, insurance, or debt costs rise, the gap between the cheapest visible price and the actual cost of participation gets wider. The person who can prepay or shift plans gets one result. The person who needs certainty pays more.

The same pattern shows up in everyday finance. A household can be “included” in the sense that it has accounts and cards, while still being pushed into a worse position by gas bills, travel prices, and food costs. That is why financial inclusion measured only by account ownership misses the point. The more relevant test is whether people can survive the shocks that turn a normal month into debt.

## 3) Platform power, labor, and the app layer

The labor story this week is not subtle. Reuters said the International Labour Organization has started final talks on binding standards for platform workers in ride-hailing, food delivery, and e-commerce.[^5] AP reported that Massachusetts ride-share drivers formed the first certified statewide U.S. union for Uber and Lyft drivers, covering nearly 70,000 contractors.[^6] Put those together and the direction is clear: workers are no longer treating the app as a neutral middleman. They are treating it as the site of power.

That matters because platforms shape the market on three levels at once. They set the visible price, they control access to demand, and they decide who gets what work. A driver, courier, or seller can be “independent” on paper while still being boxed in by rating systems, routing rules, and take rates. Once that structure is in place, the old labor categories start to wobble. The worker looks like a contractor, but the platform behaves like an employer when it wants control and like a marketplace when it wants distance.

The antitrust side is moving too. AP said the Supreme Court left in place an order holding Apple in contempt in the Epic Games case, which keeps the fight over App Store fees alive.[^7] Reuters also reported that Uber is considering a higher bid for Delivery Hero, which would push the food-delivery market toward fewer large players.[^8] So the app layer is under pressure from both ends: labor is organizing against the rules of work, and regulators are still testing how much toll a platform can charge before it starts to look like rent.

## 4) Tech, AI, and the new political economy

AI is now past the “nice demo” stage and into the part of the economy where ownership and control matter. Reuters reported that Anthropic has filed confidentially for a U.S. IPO, with AP noting a $965 billion valuation and a $47 billion annualized revenue run rate.[^9] Reuters also said law firm Kirkland & Ellis plans to spend $500 million on its own AI platform.[^10] Those two stories point in the same direction. The value is shifting from generic model access to the systems around it: workflow design, data access, review, and liability.

The labor market signal is less dramatic than the hype cycle says. Reuters reported that Bridgewater sees near-term AI-driven job losses as limited, with adoption still below 20% of U.S. firms over a recent two-week window.[^11] That does not mean AI is harmless. It means the first wave is more likely to change who does the work, who checks it, and who owns the process than to wipe out jobs overnight. The gain goes first to firms that can plug AI into existing systems and capture the savings inside their own balance sheets.

Governments are reacting by tightening the rules around data and control. Reuters reported that China has issued sweeping new outbound-investment rules covering overseas deals involving Chinese investors, technology, data, and national security.[^12] That is a reminder that AI is not just a product market. It is also a policy field about data flows, ownership, and where the surplus lands.

The week’s pattern is straightforward. Access keeps widening at the entry point, but the costs keep reappearing deeper in the system. In courts that means backlog and geography. In consumer markets it means fees, timing, and price tiers. In platform work it means control without formal employment. In AI it means capital, data, and workflow ownership. The surface gets smoother. The bargaining position does not.
