# Daily Intelligence — 2026-05-22

The week’s main signal is that access is being rationed through infrastructure, not rhetoric. Central banks, courts, and platforms are all tightening the terms around money movement, legal process, and distribution. The people paying are usually the ones with the least room to absorb delay or fees.

## 1) Rates, rails, and who gets into the system

G7 finance ministers said they need action on economic imbalances, and bond markets are still pricing a messy mix of AI capex, oil-driven inflation, and higher yields.[^1][^2] Nomura now expects the U.S. Federal Reserve to leave rates unchanged in 2026, which says a lot about how little room the central bank has to treat the inflation pulse as a short-lived wobble.[^3] When rates stay high, every downstream cost line gets sharper: food transport, credit card balances, housing rollovers, and sovereign refinancing all get pulled tighter.

That matters for inclusion because the real fight is not over apps. The Fed proposed limited payment accounts for fintechs that would let them use its rails without intraday credit, the discount window, or interest on reserves, while Trump told regulators to review whether fintechs should get wider access.[^4][^5] JPMorgan then opened its Chase digital retail bank in Germany, which shows the big banks still want the retail front door as much as the wholesale back end.[^6] In Africa, the same logic appears at sovereign scale: leaders pushed for a rethink on risk pricing, while Macron’s first-loss guarantee idea was described as falling short of debt relief and payment pauses.[^7][^8] Inclusion without downside protection is just a thinner route into the same old squeeze.

## 2) Pricing power is moving from the shelf to the gate

Apple’s contempt fight over Epic kept pressure on App Store fees, and France’s top administrative court rejected Amazon’s challenge to minimum book delivery fees.[^9][^10] Australia then moved toward a 2% levy on Big Tech unless Meta, Google, and TikTok strike local news deals.[^11] These are different markets, but the pattern is the same: the gatekeeper charges for access, then says the charge is just how the market works.

That same pattern shows up in ordinary consumer pricing. The consumer does not just pay for the item; the user pays for ranking, delivery, defaults, and exit friction. Once a platform can set the terms around the price, it can sort demand, hold users in place, and pass policy costs to the people least able to negotiate. The legal fights matter because they are where the hidden fee stack becomes visible.

## 3) Courts decide who can wait and who cannot

The Purdue opioid case is the clearest example of justice turning into paperwork. Reuters reported that the settlement over the crisis had already generated more than $57 billion, with about $865 million set aside for individuals, yet victims still ran into record demands and filing hurdles years later.[^12] The case is not just about compensation. It is about how procedure can drain a claim long before a judge reaches the facts.

Other court stories point in the same direction. A New York judge partially granted Luigi Mangione’s bid to suppress backpack evidence after finding the search unlawful, the Philippine Supreme Court rejected a bid to block the arrest of a senator wanted by the ICC, and the U.S. Supreme Court is still sorting several Trump-related cases that touch birthright citizenship and Fed independence.[^13][^14][^15] Reuters also reported that U.S. law firm expenses rose in the first quarter, including AI investment, and that productivity fell despite strong demand.[^16] When legal work gets costlier, the people with weaker claims feel it first. They run out of time, money, or both.

## 4) AI is becoming a hiring plan, not just a product demo

Google used I/O to put AI agents into Search and roll out a cheaper Gemini model for enterprise buyers.[^17] Workday said it will keep expanding its Indian workforce while deepening AI investment, and JPMorgan said it will likely hire more AI staff and fewer bankers in some categories.[^18][^19] That is what task substitution looks like when it moves from slide decks into staffing decisions. The market is no longer asking whether AI can do the work. It is asking where the work moves when AI gets cheaper.

The reaction is already social, not just technical. Reuters described young people booing rather than cheering the AI rollout, which is crude but revealing: people are not reading this only as efficiency. They are reading it as a change in status, bargaining power, and job mix.[^20]

The same week, the same mechanism kept repeating: whoever controls the gate controls the terms. Payment rails, delivery rules, court procedure, and AI distribution are all being used to decide who enters, who pays, and who waits. The fight is no longer about whether these systems matter. It is about who gets to keep the surplus after the fee, the filing rule, or the model update has done its work.
