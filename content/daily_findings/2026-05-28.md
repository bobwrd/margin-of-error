# Daily Intelligence — 2026-05-28

The clearest thread today is that more decisions are being pushed through software before the rules around them catch up. That shows up in courts, hiring, lending, pricing, and trading. The common pattern is simple: access gets easier at the front door, but the leverage stays with whoever owns the queue, the model, or the fee schedule.

## 1) Courts are getting more self-represented filings, and AI is making that louder

Bloomberg Law reported that AI-generated pro se filings are flooding court dockets, and a new study found non-prisoner pro se cases rising from 11% of all civil cases five years ago to 16.8% today.[^1] That is not just a tech story. It is what happens when legal help is too expensive, too far away, or too slow, and people try to fill the gap with tools that can draft fast but cannot judge what a court will actually tolerate. The upside is real: a person who would have stayed out of the system can now file. The downside is also real: judges, clerks, and opposing parties absorb more messy, repetitive, and sometimes overlong work.

Colorado’s revised AI law points in the same direction. Instead of requiring bias audits, it leans on disclosure when AI materially influences consequential decisions, including access to financing, housing, healthcare, education, insurance, and government services, plus meaningful human review where commercially reasonable.[^2] That is a cleaner rule than the old version, but it still leaves the hard question untouched. Notice helps only if people can use the notice to challenge a decision, and that depends on legal aid, time, and the cost of fighting back.

## 2) Financial inclusion is still mostly about who gets the expensive stuff

The inclusion story this week is not about opening more bank accounts. It is about who can smooth cash flow without getting trapped. In Laos, financial inclusion remains one of the country’s biggest problems, even as QR-payment links with Thailand expand and small businesses get easier cross-border payments.[^3] FinTech Futures also profiled earned wage access products that position themselves as an alternative to payday lenders, with one founder arguing that legacy lenders charge high fees because they cannot verify income in real time.[^4] That framing matters. A better rail can still carry a bad product.

The economics are blunt. For households living paycheck to paycheck, the real product is liquidity, not a polished app. If the fee is too high, or the repayment timing is too tight, digital credit just repackages the same stress in a cleaner wrapper. That is why financial inclusion beyond bank accounts has to mean more than access. It has to mean terms that do not turn every short cash gap into a long debt tail.

## 3) Everyday microeconomics keeps shifting from sticker price to hidden pricing logic

Airline pricing is a good example. Forbes described how AI is moving airlines toward more personalized pricing, even as predictive maintenance may reduce delays and downtime.[^5] The maintenance side is easy to like. The pricing side is not. Once an airline can use more data to sort passengers by willingness to pay, the fare you see becomes less like a market price and more like a negotiated toll. The passenger still sees one number, but the airline is increasingly pricing the person, not the seat.

That same logic sits behind the political fight over ride-hailing. Bloomberg Law reported that a House panel approved language in a transportation bill that would protect apps such as Uber from liability for crimes and negligence by drivers.[^6] If that survives, the platform keeps the upside of being the market’s organizer while pushing some of the downside elsewhere. It is the same move as app fees, delivery take rates, and subscription bundles: bundle the service, hide the margins, and make the user feel like they chose convenience when they really chose a fee stack.

## 4) AI governance is moving from abstract ethics to control rights

Robinhood’s move to let customers deploy AI agents to trade stocks and make credit card purchases is the cleanest example of where this is headed.[^7] The company is handing delegated authority to software in one of the most regulated parts of consumer finance. Reuters also noted that only 21% of business leaders surveyed by Deloitte said they had a mature governance model for agentic AI. So the question is no longer whether the system can act. It is who is responsible when it does, and whether anyone can reconstruct the decision after the fact.

Pope Leo’s new encyclical makes a similar point from a very different place. The 42,300-word text warns that AI power is concentrating in a few hands and calls for robust legal frameworks, independent oversight, informed users, and a political system that does not step back.[^8] Strip away the theology and the economics are plain. Whoever controls the data, the model, and the audit trail gets the surplus. Whoever lacks those controls gets the noise, the risk, or the bill.

Across the week, the pattern is stable. More people can file, borrow, buy, or delegate with software in the loop, but the hard power sits behind the interface: in the review process, the pricing engine, the settlement rule, or the model owner. That is where the next round of conflict is headed.
