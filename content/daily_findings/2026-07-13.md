# Daily Intelligence — 2026-07-13

Date: 2026-07-13

The biggest story in the background this week is that prices are being pushed up by more than one engine at once. Oil is jumping again, the AI buildout is chewing through power and chips, and central bankers are still talking about inflation as the main risk. That combination matters because it raises the cost of almost everything else people do, from borrowing to shipping to computing.

## Macro pressure: inflation, energy, and the AI bill

The Fed’s latest read and Christopher Waller’s comments point in the same direction: inflation is still the problem, and the labor market is not weak enough to force a quick policy pivot. Waller said the main risk is still high inflation, with unemployment at 4.2%. That is a useful setup for the rest of the week because it explains why rate cuts are not doing the work people want them to do. The central bank can slow demand, but it cannot undo tariff pressure, war-related energy shocks, or the cost of building AI infrastructure.

That AI bill is showing up in the power system. The EIA said U.S. electricity use will hit record highs in 2026 and 2027 as data centers and electrification keep demand high. Its forecast has coal’s share falling from 17% in 2025 to 15% in 2026 and 2027, while renewables rise from about 24% to 27%. That sounds like a clean energy transition on paper. In practice, it also means heavier strain on grids, more demand for transformers and transmission, and more competition between households, industry, and data centers for the same power.

Oil added another layer. Prices jumped more than 3% after U.S. and Iranian strikes, even as Trump said the Strait of Hormuz remained open to commercial traffic. Markets do not need the strait to close to price in risk; they only need the chance that shipping gets slower, insurance gets pricier, or another round of attacks lands. That is how geopolitics reaches household budgets. The transmission path runs through fuel, freight, food, and rates.

## Platform pricing: who controls the tollbooth

Apple’s price hikes on iPads and MacBooks are the cleanest example of cost pass-through. The company said memory and storage chip costs tied to the AI datacenter buildout were too high to absorb. This is the same pattern as airline fuel surcharges and ride-hailing surge pricing. The product looks stable on the front end, but the hidden costs move around behind it until the final bill reaches the buyer.

The court and regulator side is pushing back on that model. Britain’s competition regulator proposed letting app developers steer users to payment options outside Apple and Google’s app stores, and the EU court rejected Apple’s challenge to the bloc’s gatekeeper rules. The issue is not whether app stores are useful. They are. The issue is whether control over the default payment path lets one firm tax everyone else’s access. Once that path is locked in, the fee becomes part of the infrastructure, which makes it harder to see and harder to contest.

Uber’s pause on most of its planned food delivery expansion in Europe fits the same pattern. A platform can look like it is growing everywhere, but the economics can get tight fast once the market is already split and the deal flow shifts toward consolidation. Platform firms keep trying to own the route between user and merchant because that is where the fee lives. When regulators or rivals narrow that route, the pricing power gets less stable.

## Courts, paperwork, and access to justice

The most interesting legal story in the scan is not one single verdict. It is the way procedure keeps deciding who can participate. A federal appeals court said migrants cannot be held without bond hearings past 90 days, and AP reported people camping outside a Utah courthouse just to get scarce public seats in a high-profile hearing. Those are different settings, but the economics are the same. Time is a cost, and court systems ration time through hearings, queues, filing rules, and physical access.

Spain’s migrant legalization drive is another version of the same thing. The country received about 900,000 applications, roughly double expectations. That tells you how many people are trying to move from informal status into something legible to the state. Once they are in that process, the costs are not just legal fees. They are transport, waiting time, lost wages, language barriers, and the risk of being stuck outside the system long enough to lose work or housing.

Student loan changes in the U.S. cut in a similar direction. Millions of borrowers are moving into a less forgiving repayment setup, while Parent PLUS borrowers taking new loans after July 1 lose access to income-driven repayment options. It is not court procedure, but it is still a state-designed queue. The person with the least margin ends up absorbing the deadline risk, the paperwork risk, and the repayment shock. That is how legal design becomes a balance-sheet problem.

## Credit, inclusion, and the price of access

Financial inclusion still gets talked about as if the main question is whether someone has an account. The more useful question is what happens after access is granted. Reuters’ analysis of 53 publicly traded business development companies is a warning: the sector turned unprofitable as loan losses and debt costs rose. That is the broader credit market in miniature. Lending can still happen, but once funding costs climb, the borrower pays for the system’s stress.

The newest consumer credit products often work by hiding that stress in plain sight. AP’s report on rent-now, pay-later companies makes the same point from a different angle: users may not understand the fee structure until the bill has already moved past the original rent payment. That is why inclusion and extraction are not opposites. A product can widen access and still drain value through complexity, rollover risk, or late-fee design. The difference is whether the user gets breathing room or just a longer path into debt.

Student lending also belongs in this bucket because it is a form of forced long-duration credit. The policy shift on July 1 does not just change repayment math. It changes who can manage shocks and who cannot. A borrower with savings can absorb a worse term sheet. A borrower without savings gets pushed closer to delinquency, which is exactly where “access” stops feeling like access and starts feeling like a trap.

## AI, labor, and the new political economy

AI is now moving through two labor markets at once. In India, hiring for AI roles outpaced overall IT recruitment last month, while Microsoft cut about 4,800 jobs as it shifted resources toward AI infrastructure. That is the split screen to watch. Some workers get pulled into higher-value tasks. Others get cut as firms rework the org chart around automation and capex. The productivity story is real, but so is the pressure to do more with fewer people.

The policy response is lagging behind the market shift. The Bank of England said AI poses growing financial-stability risk, and China warned about a backdoor risk in Anthropic’s Claude Code. A legal tech firm also sued the U.S. after rules limiting foreign access to top Anthropic models disrupted its Canada-based team. That is not just about safety. It is about who gets to use the tool, who owns the output, and who takes the hit when access is restricted by national-security rules.

The deeper issue is surplus capture. If model owners, chipmakers, and data-center builders take the upside while workers face tighter targets, higher monitoring, and more unstable jobs, then AI is just a new layer of market power. If the gains are shared through wages, lower prices, or better services, the story looks different. Right now the evidence points both ways, but the bottlenecks are clear: compute, energy, code access, and firm control over the platform.

The week’s pattern is simple. The economy is being shaped less by broad averages and more by chokepoints: power grids, shipping lanes, app stores, court calendars, repayment rules, and model access. That is why the same system can look efficient on paper and still feel punitive on the ground. When every important flow is gated, the people with the least time, money, or status pay first.
