# Daily Intelligence — 2026-06-01

This week’s clearest signal is that access is getting easier to request and harder to complete. Courts, banks, and platforms are all adding more front-door convenience, but the cost keeps moving into the queue, the fee schedule, or the review layer. That is where the pressure is landing.

## 1) Access to justice is still a queue problem

Bloomberg Law reported that AI-generated pro se filings are flooding dockets, with non-prisoner pro se civil cases rising from 11% of all civil cases five years ago to 16.8% now.[^1] That is a real access gain on paper. A person who could not afford counsel can file more easily, and that matters. But courts do not run on filing alone. They run on sorting, verification, and follow-up. When the volume of filings rises faster than the system’s ability to check them, the extra work falls on clerks, judges, and the other side. The result is not just more access. It is more noise.

Reuters’ report that civil rights groups sued over conditions at the largest U.S. immigration detention center points in the same direction.[^2] When people are stuck in detention, the legal process is not abstract. Travel, communication, and time all become costs. The Supreme Court’s recent decision to leave in place limits tied to immigration judges’ public speech adds another layer: the system is being managed through control of process and voice, not only through the law on the merits.[^3] That is why access to justice keeps looking like an economics story. The price is not only the filing fee. It is the waiting, the distance, and the energy spent just to keep a case alive.

## 2) Inclusion means little if the price of ordinary life keeps jumping

Reuters said the average U.S. mortgage rate rose to a nine-month high, driven in part by inflation fears and higher Treasury yields.[^4] Reuters also reported that U.S. banks posted a 3.6% profit increase in the first quarter, to $80.5 billion.[^11] Put together, the picture is plain: the financial system can still be healthy while households feel more squeezed. Bank profits do not tell you much about whether a borrower can absorb a higher monthly payment.

That same split shows up in everyday spending. Reuters reported that summer flights and hotel prices are dividing travelers into haves and have-nots, with American Airlines saying demand has taken a K-shaped pattern.[^5] The mechanism is familiar: fixed costs, capacity constraints, and price discrimination. People with money buy flexibility. Everyone else gets steered toward the cheapest option, then pays in time, stress, or worse conditions. The label on the price tag hides the real cost. That is true in housing, travel, and almost anywhere demand outstrips supply.

## 3) Platform power is showing up in labor and consolidation

Massachusetts ride-share drivers just became the first certified union of app-based drivers in the U.S., covering nearly 70,000 independent contractors.[^6] That matters because the platform is where the bargaining power sits. Drivers do not just argue over pay. They argue over access to rides, ratings, routing, and the rules that decide who gets work at all. When a market is run through an app, the app is not neutral. It is the market.

Reuters also reported that Delivery Hero shares jumped after Uber made an indicative bid, a sign that the food-delivery market keeps moving toward fewer, larger players.[^7] That kind of consolidation matters because scale changes the rules. It can lower some costs, but it also makes pricing more opaque and makes it harder for workers, merchants, and users to walk away. The more a platform controls demand, logistics, and payment flow, the easier it is to turn convenience into rent.

## 4) AI is moving from drafting text to controlling workflows

Bloomberg Law reported that Florida’s justices adopted a statewide policy warning lawyers and pro se litigants that AI misuse in filings can lead to sanctions.[^8] Bloomberg Law also reported that Kirkland & Ellis plans to spend $500 million building its own AI platform.[^9] Those two stories point to the same shift. AI is no longer treated as a toy for drafting. It is becoming part of the control stack: who checks the output, who owns the workflow, and who takes responsibility when the machine gets it wrong.

Reuters’ report that Robinhood will let customers deploy AI agents to trade stocks and make credit-card purchases makes the risk even clearer.[^10] Once software can act on a user’s behalf, the main questions are not about novelty. They are about reversal, audit, and loss allocation. If the tool can place the order, the next issue is who can unwind it. If the tool can spend money, the next issue is who eats the mistake.

The week’s pattern is consistent across all three themes. The front end keeps getting smoother, but the real power sits behind it: in the backlog, the rate, the fee, the union vote, the sanction rule, and the workflow owner. That is where the next fights are happening.
