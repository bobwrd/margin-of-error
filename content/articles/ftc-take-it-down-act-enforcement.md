## What Happened

On 19 May 2026, the Federal Trade Commission announced the commencement of enforcement proceedings under the TAKE IT DOWN Act (Public Law 118-62), signed by President Biden in April 2025. The Act — the first federal statute specifically targeting AI-generated nonconsensual intimate imagery (NCII) — imposes a mandatory 48-hour removal obligation on online platforms upon receiving a valid notice from a victim or their authorised representative. It also requires platforms to establish internal reporting mechanisms, designate NCII response teams, and maintain records of notices and responses for three years.

The FTC's enforcement action was triggered by complaints against three major platforms whose NCII response times exceeded 96 hours in at least 80% of verified cases — well beyond the statutory maximum. The FTC's complaint alleged that each platform had engaged in unfair acts or practices under Section 5 of the FTC Act by failing to establish and maintain reasonable procedures to comply with the Act's requirements. Each enforcement action carries a proposed penalty of $150 million, payable in instalments tied to compliance milestones.

## Legal Mechanism

The TAKE IT DOWN Act operates through a combination of direct obligations and FTC enforcement. Unlike GDPR's consent-and-erase framework, it does not create a private right of action — victims cannot sue platforms directly under the statute. Instead, enforcement is exclusively administrative, through the FTC. This design reflects a legislative compromise: platforms lobbied aggressively against private rights of action, while privacy advocates argued that agency enforcement would be insufficiently responsive to individual victims.

The Act's key operative provision is Section 3(b), which defines the "valid notice" standard: a notice is valid if it includes a sworn statement from the victim (or their authorised representative) identifying the content, the platform, and the manner in which it was obtained without consent. Platforms are prohibited from requiring additional verification beyond this threshold. Once a valid notice is received, the 48-hour clock begins; platforms must remove the content from all publicly accessible surfaces and prevent resharing of the same piece of content.

The FTC Act Section 5 angle is significant because it allows the Commission to bring enforcement actions for unfair acts or practices even where a specific statutory provision has not been violated — establishing that a platform's overall NCII response framework constitutes an unfair practice if it falls below the statutory standard.

## Economic and Market Implications

Compliance costs for large platforms are estimated at $2–5 billion annually in the US alone, driven primarily by staffing NCII response teams, building automated detection systems, and overhauling content moderation pipelines to meet the 48-hour removal window. The Act does not mandate specific detection technologies, but platforms with inadequate automated filters face a structural disadvantage: human review alone cannot scale to meet the 48-hour window for high-volume platforms.

The FTC's proposed $150 million penalties — while material for any individual platform — are small relative to the market capitalisation of the companies involved (Meta: ~$1.4 trillion; Google: ~$2.1 trillion). Legal observers note that the more significant risk is reputational: the FTC has released the names of the three platforms in its enforcement announcements, generating significant press coverage and signalling that NCII enforcement is an active Commission priority.

## Global Context

No jurisdiction has enacted an identical 48-hour removal statute. Australia's Online Safety Act 2021 establishes a 24-hour removal window for cyber abuse material but does not specifically address AI-generated NCII. The UK has relied on the Online Safety Act 2023's Category 1 obligations, which require platforms to take proportionate systems and processes to prevent users encountering priority content. Canada's Online Harms Act (Bill C-270) establishes a 24-hour notice-and-removal framework, though enforcement has not yet commenced.

The TAKE IT DOWN Act's 48-hour hard deadline stands out internationally as a technology-neutral, victims-rights-framed approach that delegates enforcement to an existing agency rather than creating a new regulator. It has attracted interest from Japan's Digital Agency and South Korea's Personal Information Protection Commission as a potential model.
