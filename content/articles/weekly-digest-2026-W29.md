This week’s economic story was a chain of gates. TSMC’s second-quarter profit jumped 77% as demand for AI chips stayed strong, while China reported a sharp rise in consumer defaults. In Britain, the state added more than 2,300 magistrates to cut court delays, even as legal-aid funding in North Carolina faced a major squeeze. Growth is moving, but access to the systems that deliver it is being rationed.

## The AI boom has owners before it has users

TSMC’s result shows that AI demand is real. It also shows where the first gains land: with firms that control advanced chips, packaging, cloud capacity, and the capital needed to build data centres. Federal Reserve officials warned that AI infrastructure could push up technology and electricity prices. A small software company or public agency may get better tools later, but it pays today’s higher input costs without the same ability to spread them across a global customer base.

That is a political-economy problem, not only an investment story. If productivity gains arrive after the bills, owners of scarce infrastructure capture the early surplus while users absorb the transition cost.

## Credit access can turn a pay cut into a trap

Reuters described a Chinese telecoms worker carrying about 140,000 yuan in debt across credit cards, online loans, and a car loan after a pay cut and the loss of a fuel allowance. China Merchants Bank’s personal-loan non-performing-loan ratio reached 1.14% in the first quarter, up 0.13 percentage point from a year earlier. Each loan may have looked manageable alone. The household faces the combined repayment schedule.

An account or instant approval is therefore a weak measure of inclusion. The better question is whether emergency credit remains survivable after income falls. Digital lenders make borrowing quick, but they do not always make total exposure visible. Several small claims can become fixed monthly payments, turning a temporary shock into a default spiral.

## Courts have capacity, but claimants still need a way in

The UK’s new magistrates may increase the number of cases processed, but court capacity is only one bottleneck. North Carolina’s budget effectively removed a major source of funding for civil legal aid. Pisgah Legal Services said the Interest on Lawyers’ Trust Accounts programme had supplied $1.9 million, about 15% of its annual operating budget. The affected cases include eviction, foreclosure, domestic violence, and protection orders.

A court can recognise a right while a claimant loses housing, wages, or safety before the hearing. People with money can pay for advice and survive adjournments. People without it face procedural risk as a direct economic cost. California’s proposal to let supervised, trained nonlawyers handle limited legal work is worth watching because it targets that preparation gap, not only the queue.

## Platforms keep the tollbooth even when the fee falls

The UK’s proposed app-store rules and the reported $14.8 billion Uber offer for Delivery Hero point to the same issue. A lower payment commission does not remove platform power if Apple, Google, or a food-delivery app still controls discovery, ranking, data, and the default route to customers. Restaurants and developers depend on the network because users are already there.

The same design shapes everyday prices. Delivery apps may reveal service and small-order charges late; ride-hailing prices move with demand and driver supply. Dynamic pricing can reflect real scarcity, but users need the full price early and a realistic way to switch. Otherwise limited attention becomes another input the platform can monetise.

Next week, watch whether regulators target the gates themselves: portability of user data, access to alternative payment routes, legal-aid funding, and ownership of compute and power. The week’s common measure is bargaining power under stress. Who can wait, compare, appeal, or switch when the intermediary changes the price or the rules?
