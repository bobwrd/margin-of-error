## What Happened

A California federal jury unanimously rejected all claims by Elon Musk against OpenAI, Sam Altman, Greg Brockman, and Microsoft, finding that Musk’s claims alleging breach of charitable trust and unjust enrichment from OpenAI’s conversion to a for-profit structure were time-barred under applicable statutes of limitation. Musk has indicated intent to appeal to the Ninth Circuit.

## Legal Mechanism

U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California. Common-law charitable trust doctrine; unjust enrichment. Applicable SoL: three years (breach of charitable trust, Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 338(a)) and two years (unjust enrichment). The court did not rule on the merits.

## Economic and Market Implications

OpenAI avoids potential restructuring or damages exposure of up to $150B. The verdict greenlights the existing for-profit restructuring model for AI companies that originated as nonprofits. Microsoft was dismissed as a matter of law.

## Global Context

United States is not acting in isolation. The EU AI Act has already triggered regulatory copycat effects in Brazil, Canada, Singapore, and the UK, as jurisdictions race to draft comparable frameworks. In the US, federal AI regulation remains fragmented across sector-specific agencies — the FTC, CFPB, FDA, and HHS each applying their own statutory authority — but the direction of travel is consistent: more mandatory obligations, more enforcement, and higher penalties for non-compliance. The compliance burden falls most heavily on smaller firms and foreign companies without dedicated EU legal teams.

The AI governance landscape is consolidating around three poles: the EU's comprehensive risk-tiered approach, the US's sector-agency approach, and China's algorithmic recommendation and generative AI rules. For companies operating globally, this means segmenting AI systems by jurisdiction and applying the strictest applicable standard as the de facto baseline.
