This week’s clearest pattern is that access is no longer a yes-or-no question. It is being priced, delayed, or routed through a gate in courts, consumer apps, and AI systems. Once that happens, the people with more time, money, and leverage keep moving. Everyone else pays to wait.

## Courts are turning delay into a cost

The UK’s employment tribunal backlog has reached 531,000 cases. That number is not just a court statistic. It is a price tag on time. A claim that takes months or years to move loses value while the party with more cash can keep paying lawyers, keep records in order, and wait for the other side to crack. That is why backlog is not a neutral admin problem. It changes who can use the law at all.

The same logic shows up in legal aid. North Carolina’s budget cut support that has helped low-income residents for decades, including money routed through IOLTA. On paper, that is a line item. In practice, it means more people show up in family court or eviction cases without a lawyer. Once that happens, procedure starts doing more of the work than merit. Miss one deadline, miss one filing, and the case can turn before anyone hears the facts.

## Platforms are learning to charge by path, not just by price

The most interesting consumer pricing story of the week is not a simple fare increase. It is the way platforms split the same service into different experiences and different charges. Consumer Reports found that Uber and Lyft users could see sharply different prices for similar rides at similar times. That suggests the app is testing willingness to pay, not just responding to supply and demand. The customer is part of the pricing model.

That same logic runs through app stores and payment systems. When a platform controls the default path, it can make one route feel normal and another feel like a detour. The UK’s move to let app developers steer users toward outside payment options is one answer to that problem. So is the pressure on Google and Apple’s gatekeeping rules in Europe and Brazil. The issue is not only the fee. It is the power to decide which fee the user ever sees.

Buy-now-pay-later shows the softer version of the same trick. It makes the sticker price feel smaller by splitting the pain across months. That can help a buyer breathe, but it can also hide how much debt is being stacked into an ordinary budget. Inclusion and overextension can look very similar at checkout.

## AI is moving from a product story to a balance-sheet story

The Bank of England’s warning this week was useful because it treated AI as a stability issue, not just a software story. That is the right frame. AI now affects capital spending, chip demand, cloud bills, and the pricing of services built on top of it. If the bet pays off slowly, the costs still land now.

The policy response is starting to catch up. Illinois has set out a frontier AI law with disclosure, audits, and incident reporting. The Bureau of Labor Statistics is also asking how to measure worker AI use in the time-use survey. That matters because you cannot govern what you refuse to measure. If AI is changing task mix, pace, or staffing, the evidence has to show up somewhere before the market calls it productivity and moves on.

## What to watch next week

The next test is whether these bottlenecks get tighter or easier to cross. Watch for more court fixes aimed at delay, more pricing tricks that split one service into many payments, and more AI rules that ask firms to prove what their systems actually do. The week’s real story is not that markets are closed. It is that access now comes with terms, and those terms decide who gets heard first.
