On 1 June 2026, the European Commission said it had appointed a Scientific Panel and an Advisory Forum to support enforcement of the AI Act. That is not a new ban, a new deadline, or a fresh fine. It is the part of the regime that decides whether the law can actually be applied without becoming mush.

The move matters because the AI Act is a classification law as much as it is a prohibition law. A lot turns on whether a model is treated as general-purpose AI, whether a system falls into a high-risk bucket, and whether a deployer can argue it is just adjacent to the regulated use case. The Commission is adding more formal technical support around those calls.

## What changed

The Scientific Panel brings 60 independent experts. Its job is narrow in a useful way. It advises the AI Office and national authorities on GPAI models, systemic risk, model classification, evaluation methods, and cross-border market surveillance. The Advisory Forum is broader. It includes academia, civil society, industry, SMEs, startups, ENISA, the EU Agency for Fundamental Rights, and standardisation bodies.

That mix is doing two jobs at once. First, it gives the Commission better technical input. Second, it creates a more legible enforcement process for everyone outside Brussels. Companies no longer get to pretend the rules are being applied in a vacuum by people with no specialist help. The EU is building a thicker institutional wrapper around the text.

## Why the AI Act needed this

The AI Act leaves a lot of room for interpretation at the edges. That was always true. The hard questions are not about obvious banned uses. They are about borderline systems. Is a model general-purpose but harmless enough to sit in the background? Is a system high-risk because of what it does, or only because of where it is used? Does a performance tool become a regulated system when it nudges hiring, credit, education, or law-enforcement decisions?

Those are not academic questions. They decide who has to document, test, notify, monitor, and keep records. They also decide which firms can get away with a narrow reading of the rules and which ones cannot. The new panel and forum are meant to reduce that uncertainty before it becomes inconsistent enforcement.

## Legal mechanics

The Commission says the Scientific Panel was established under Article 68 of the AI Act, together with Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2025/454. The governance page lays out the structure clearly: the European AI Office, national market surveillance authorities, the European Artificial Intelligence Board, the Scientific Panel, and the Advisory Forum all sit inside the enforcement architecture.

That is the core story. The AI Act is formally shared between EU and national levels, but the Commission still needs specialist help when systems cross borders or when a model’s risk profile is not obvious. The panel helps with the technical side. The forum helps with the stakeholder side. Together they make the regime harder to shrug off as just political theater.

## What this means in practice

For companies, the immediate effect is not more paperwork from the Commission. It is less room for doubt. A stronger expert structure usually means tighter classification, faster convergence on standards, and fewer comfortable loopholes. If you were hoping to argue that the law is too vague to apply, this is bad news.

For regulators, the benefit is consistency. If member states are left to interpret the same model in very different ways, the EU ends up with a patchwork regime. The new bodies make that less likely. They also make it easier for the AI Office to lean on technical advice when a case is ugly or politically sensitive.

The practical result is modest but real: the AI Act just got a more serious enforcement spine. Not a bigger headline. A better machine.
