## What Happened

On 1 August 2024, Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 — the EU Artificial Intelligence Act — entered into force, becoming the world's first comprehensive horizontal AI regulation. It establishes a four-tier risk pyramid: unacceptable risk (banned outright), high risk (mandatory conformity assessments required before market placement), limited risk (transparency obligations only), and minimal risk (no obligations). The Act applies to any provider or deployer operating within the EU market, regardless of where they are incorporated.

The prohibition list — covering real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, social scoring by public authorities, and AI systems that manipulate human behaviour through subliminal techniques — took effect in February 2025. General-purpose AI model obligations (Article 51) applied from August 2025, requiring providers to disclose training data, publish technical documentation, and comply with copyright directives. High-risk system obligations — the most operationally demanding tier — were initially due August 2026, later pushed to December 2027 by the 2026 Omnibus amendment for certain Annex III use cases.

Enforcement is led by national market surveillance authorities in each member state, coordinated by the EU AI Office established within the Commission. Penalties are deliberately severe: up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover for prohibited practice violations; up to €15 million or 3% for other violations.

## Legal Mechanism

The Act was adopted under Article 114 TFEU (internal market harmonisation), giving it direct effect across all 27 member states without requiring national transposition legislation. It operates as a Regulation — binding in its entirety — rather than a Directive, which means the compliance architecture is uniform from Dublin to Athens.

The core classification gate is Article 6: an AI system is high-risk if it falls within one of the eight Annex III use cases (including biometric ID, critical infrastructure management, education, employment, essential services, law enforcement, migration, and administration of justice) AND is placed on the market or put into service in the EU. Providers of GPAI models with systemic risk (compute threshold >10²⁵ FLOPs) face additional obligations including adversarial testing, incident reporting, and model-specific cybersecurity policies.

## Economic and Market Implications

Compliance costs for high-risk AI systems are substantial: initial conformity assessment alone is estimated at €400,000–€1.2 million per system, before ongoing audit costs. The EU AI Office projects a net GDP impact of +€271 billion over five years through productivity gains, partially offset by near-term compliance drag on EU-headquartered AI firms. The Brussels Effect — the EU's ability to set de facto global standards — means that companies building AI for EU deployment effectively must apply the Act's requirements globally.

A new AI compliance industry is already forming: notified bodies, compliance auditors, legal advisors, and AI governance software vendors are scaling to meet demand. Market size for this sector is projected at €2–4 billion by 2027. The Act also creates a two-track compliance path for non-EU companies: either segment EU AI deployments (operationally expensive) or standardise globally on EU requirements (costly upfront, but simpler to maintain).

## Global Context

The EU AI Act is not operating in isolation. Brazil's AI bill (PL 2338/2023) mirrors the risk-tiered approach. Canada's proposed AI and Data Act (AIDA) follows a similar architecture. Singapore's Model AI Governance Framework and AI Verify tool were explicitly designed to help Singaporean firms demonstrate EU compliance. The UK's approach remains sector-specific through existing regulators — FCA, ICO, Ofcom — rather than horizontal, but the regulatory intent is converging.

In the United States, federal AI regulation remains fragmented. The FTC applies its existing Section 5 authority to unfair and deceptive practices. The FDA regulates AI in medical devices. The CFPB and HUD apply their statutory mandates to AI in credit and housing. No comprehensive federal AI law exists. The absence of a US federal equivalent means the Brussels Effect operates most forcefully on companies with global operations: the EU's definitions and documentation requirements are becoming the global default simply because no other jurisdiction has published equivalently detailed rules.
