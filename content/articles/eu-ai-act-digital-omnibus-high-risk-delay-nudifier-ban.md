## What Happened

On 15 February 2026, the European Commission published its much-anticipated *Digital Decade* amendment package — colloquially the "AI Act Digital Omnibus" — which proposed targeted modifications to Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 across four areas. The most consequential changes were: (1) a 14-month extension of the high-risk conformity assessment deadline from August 2026 to December 2027 for a subset of Annex III use cases; (2) the introduction of a new prohibition on AI systems designed to alter the behaviour of persons in workplaces or educational institutions through subliminal or deceptive techniques ("nudifier systems"); (3) clarification that general-purpose AI model providers retain sole responsibility for GPAI compliance, without downstream integrator obligations; and (4) a new mandatory incident reporting obligation for GPAI providers, requiring disclosure of serious incidents within 72 hours of detection.

The Omnibus was adopted under the Commission's delegated power to amend Annex III (the high-risk use case list) and to update the Act's implementation timeline, without requiring full legislative passage through the European Parliament and Council — a procedure that had caused the original Act's lengthy 2021–2024 legislative journey.

The nudifier ban was the most politically sensitive element. "Nudifier systems" — AI applications that deliberately use subliminal manipulation, deceptive design, or targeted psychological techniques to shift user behaviour — had been flagged by the EU AI Office as a gap in the original Act's prohibition Annex. The Omnibus added these to the prohibited list, with a 6-month implementation grace period for systems already deployed.

## Legal Mechanism

The Omnibus exercises the Commission's delegated powers under Article 4(3) of the AI Act, which allows the Commission to supplement the list of prohibited AI practices by delegated act where three conditions are met: the AI practice poses a clear risk to health, safety, or fundamental rights; the risk is specific and identifiable; and no existing provision adequately addresses it. The nudifier ban was added to Annex I (the prohibited practices list) via Delegated Regulation (EU) 2026/342.

The high-risk delay operates through an amendment to Annex IX of the Act, extending the transition period for conformity assessments for certain Annex III use cases. This is not a general extension — it applies only to AI systems used in: (a) employment decisions and worker management; (b) management of critical infrastructure; and (c) certain education and vocational training contexts. AI systems in high-stakes domains like credit scoring, medical devices, and law enforcement were not covered by the delay.

The GPAI incident reporting obligation is the most operationally demanding new provision: GPAI providers must report "serious incidents" — defined as any malfunction, misuse, or adverse event that has caused or could reasonably be expected to cause harm to fundamental rights, health, or safety — within 72 hours of the provider becoming aware. This is analogous to the GDPR breach notification timeline, applied to AI-specific failure modes.

## Economic and Market Implications

The 14-month delay is a significant relief for EU-based AI vendors and their customers. The conformity assessment process for high-risk AI systems is lengthy: notified bodies are in short supply, and the queue for assessments was projected to extend to 18–24 months even before the Act came into force. A conformity assessment that began in January 2026 would not have concluded before August 2026 under the original timeline; the December 2027 extension brings the deadline within a realistic operational planning horizon.

The nudifier ban has immediate commercial implications for a sector of the EU's behavioural design and gamification software industry estimated at €800 million in annual revenue. Most of these tools operated in HR tech, edtech, and consumer app contexts. The 6-month grace period provides a window for product redesign — shifting from subliminal manipulation techniques to transparent, consent-based approaches — but vendors that cannot adapt will need to exit the EU market.

The GPAI incident reporting obligation imposes a new compliance cost on OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, and Mistral (the primary GPAI model providers operating in the EU), who must now establish incident detection and reporting pipelines that can deliver 72-hour notifications. The definition of "serious incident" remains contested; the EU AI Office has issued interpretive guidance, but finalised guidelines are not expected until Q3 2026.

## Global Context

The Omnibus illustrates the EU's iterative approach to AI governance: rather than waiting for a full legislative revision cycle, the Commission uses delegated regulation to update the Act's scope and timeline. This approach has drawn criticism from Parliament members who argue that significant policy changes — like the nudifier ban — should require democratic deliberation rather than Commission decision. The European Parliament has reserved the right to object to delegated regulations within a two-month scrutiny period.

For non-EU companies, the practical effect of the Omnibus is to extend the compliance runway for certain high-risk use cases while tightening requirements for GPAI providers. The nudifier ban creates a de facto global standard: companies building behavioural design tools for EU users must redesign for transparency regardless of where they are based, given the EU market's size.
