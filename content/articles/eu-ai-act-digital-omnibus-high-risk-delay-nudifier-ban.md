## What Happened

EU Coreper approved a provisional agreement on the AI Act Digital Omnibus (Omnibus VII), negotiated 7 May 2026, which postpones high-risk AI obligations from August 2026 to December 2027 (Annex III systems) and August 2028 (Annex I product-safety systems). The package also adds a new explicit ban on AI 'nudifier' apps, effective December 2026.

## Legal Mechanism

Amendment to Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 via Digital Omnibus (Omnibus VII). Political agreement 7 May 2026; Coreper approval 13 May 2026; Parliament plenary vote expected June 2026; Council formal adoption expected before August 2026. Directly applicable across all 27 EU member states.

## Economic and Market Implications

The 16-month delay provides near-term regulatory relief for thousands of EU businesses deploying high-risk AI in hiring, healthcare, finance, and law enforcement. Compliance cost deferral estimated €500K–€2M per affected firm. The nudifier ban forces AI image/video companies to build content moderation infrastructure by December 2026.

## Global Context

European Union is not acting in isolation. The EU AI Act has already triggered regulatory copycat effects in Brazil, Canada, Singapore, and the UK, as jurisdictions race to draft comparable frameworks. In the US, federal AI regulation remains fragmented across sector-specific agencies — the FTC, CFPB, FDA, and HHS each applying their own statutory authority — but the direction of travel is consistent: more mandatory obligations, more enforcement, and higher penalties for non-compliance. The compliance burden falls most heavily on smaller firms and foreign companies without dedicated EU legal teams.

The AI governance landscape is consolidating around three poles: the EU's comprehensive risk-tiered approach, the US's sector-agency approach, and China's algorithmic recommendation and generative AI rules. For companies operating globally, this means segmenting AI systems by jurisdiction and applying the strictest applicable standard as the de facto baseline.
